{% raw %}IV. Passports. Custom-House. Luggage.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Passports, though not required in Italy, are occasionally useful.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Registered letters, for example, will not be delivered to strangers,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unless they exhibit a passport to prove their identity. The count¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enance and help of the English and American consuls can, of course,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be extended to those persons only who can prove their nationality.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In thn remote neighbourhoods, too, where the public safety de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mands a more rigorous supervision, the traveller is sometimes asked{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for his credentials, but this remark is scarely necessary in regard to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the districts embraced in this volume of the Handbook. The Italian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}police authorities are generally civil and obliging.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Custom-House. The examination of luggage at the Italian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}custom-houses is generally lenient. Tobacco and cigars are the ar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ticles chiefly sought for. At the gates of most of the Italian towns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a tax (dazio consumo) is levied on comestibles, but travellers'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}luggage is passed at the barriers (limite daziario) on a simple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}declaration that it contains no such articles.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Luggage. If possible, luggage should never be sent to Italy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by goods-train, as it is liable to damage , pilferage, and undue{% endraw %}
{% raw %}custom-house detention. If the traveller is obliged to forward it in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this way, he should employ a trustworthy agent at the frontier and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}send him the keys. As a rule it is advisable, and often in the end{% endraw %}
{% raw %}less expensive , never to part from one's luggage, and to super¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intend the custom-house examination in person.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}V. Beggars.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Begging, which was countenanced and encouraged under the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}old system of Italian politics, still continues to be one of those na¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tional nuisances to which the traveller must habituate himself. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}present government has adopted energetic measures for its suppres¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sion, but hitherto with only partial success. The average Italian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beggar is a mere speculator, and not a deserving object of charity.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The traveller should therefore decline to give anything, with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}words, 'non e'e niente', or a gesture of refusal. If a donation be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bestowed, it should consist of the smallest possible copper coin.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A beggar, who on one occasion was presented with 2c. and thanked{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the donor with the usual benedictions, was on another presented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with 50 c. , but this act of liberality, instead of being gratefully{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accepted, only called forth the remark in a half-offended tone;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Ma, Signore, e molto poco !'{% endraw %}
