{% raw %}The "Cappella S. Jacopo e Cristoforo, adjoining the right transept,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is embellished with celebrated frescoes, forming one of the finest existing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}specimens of Upper Italian art. Although now considerably damaged,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these pictures are still very attractive, while their decorative parts show{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that the School of Squarcione, from which they emanated, was indebted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for many suggestions to its study of the antique. The Evangelists on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceiling are the poorest, and probably the earliest part of the work. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}four upper sections on the wall on the right are also by inferior artists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the St. Christopher with the Infant Christ is by Bono; the two highest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scenes, representing St. James as a worker of miracles, and St. James be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fore the king, are by an unknown master (Zoppo ?); the adoration of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}giant saint (central section on the right) is by Ansuino da Forli. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paintings on the wall and vaulting of the recesses of the choir, are by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Niccolb Pizzolo, an able master of Padua, who died young. By far the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most important of all these works are the "Pictures with which Andrea{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mantegna completed the cycle in the second half of the 15th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The left wall presents to us the life of St. James from his calling to his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}execution. The lower scenes exhibit greater ability and maturity than the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}upper, so that we can almost trace the master's progress step by step.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Execution and Burial of St. Christopher, the last pictures on the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall, also by Mantegna, are sadly injured. — The large altar-relief of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Madonna and saints in terracotta, by Giov. da Pisa, a pupil of Donatello{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who worked for a considerable time at Padua, has visibly influenced the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painters of the frescoes.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The chapel to the right of the high-altar contains a Coronation of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mary of the school of Giotto.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Sacristy (entrance to the left of the choir) contains an altar-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}piece by Guido Reni (covered), representing John the Baptist, and a mon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}umental relief of a mourning female figure, by Canova.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the N. side of the Piazza in front of the church is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entrance (if closed, ring at the large wooden gate; fee i/2 fr.) to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the *Madonna dell' Arena (Annunziata, PL 2; G, 4), situated in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an oval garden which shows the outlines of an ancient amphi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}theatre. The chapel, which is oblong in form, was erected by Scro-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vegno, a Paduan architect, in 1303, and its walls and vaulting are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}completely covered with a series of **Frescoes by Giotto, most of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which are well preserved (restored by Botti). The period of their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}execution is determined by the fact that Dante and Giotto met at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Padua in 1306. Morning-light is the most favourable.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}These frescoes represent the History of the Virgin and Christ, as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}narrated in the apocryphal Proto-Evangelium and in the New Testament,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and end, according to ancient custom, with the Last Judgment painted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the entrance-wall. The last work is much injured, and was probably{% endraw %}
{% raw %}executed more by Giotto's pupils than by the master himself, whose hand{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is unmistakeably revealed only in the graceful figure of Christ at the top,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surrounded by apostles, angels, and saints. The paintings on the side-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls are arranged in four rows, one above another. The Uppermost{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Row (beginning to the right of the choir-arch) relates the history of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Virgin from the rejection of Joachim's sacrifice to Mary's bridal pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cession. The Birth of the Virgin and the Presentation of the Virgin in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Temple are marked by much delicacy of treatment. — The Second{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Row begins with the Annunciation (choir-arch), and depicts the youth of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christ and the events of his ministry up to the driving of the money¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}changers out of the Temple. The finest scenes are the Adoration of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Magi, the Flight into Egypt, and the Entry into Jerusalem. — The highest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}flight of Giotto's imagination is seen in some of the paintings in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Third Row, which is mainly occupied with the scenes of the Passion.{% endraw %}
