{% raw %}Ferrara is situated near the ancient Forum Alieni, 3!/2 M. to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. of the Po, in the midst of a fertile, but unhealthy plain. It is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}capital of a Delegation, with 29,000 inhab. (commune 77,000),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and possesses broad, deserted streets, mouldering palaces, and other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}imposing reminiscences of its golden period. It was once a prosper¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ous commercial place, numbering 100,000 inhab., and was the seat{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the renowned court of the House of Este.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The family of Este was descended from the Lombardic invaders of Italy,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and derived its feudal name from the castle mentioned at p. 300. Azzo I.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}became Count or Margrave of Este under Emp. Henry III. His eldest son{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Welf (founder of the younger branch of the Guelphs) was invested with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Duchy of Bavaria, which had belonged to his grandfather, the last male{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representative of the elder branch of the Guelphs, and his son Henry the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Proud became the founder of the families of Brunswick and Hanover.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Giulio, the second son of Welf, was the ancestor of the dukes of Ferrara{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Modena. Obizzo III., who added Modena and Reggio to his dominions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(d. 1352), considerably extended the power of his house, which from an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}early period was a liberal patron of art and science. In 1452 Borso{% endraw %}
{% raw %}received the title of Duke of Modena and Reggio from Emp. Frederick III.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and that of Duke of Ferrara from Pope Paul II. He died in 1471. His{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brother Hercules I. (1471-1505), and the son of the latter, Alphonso I.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1505-34), husband of the infamous Lucrezia Borgia, were powerful and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}influential princes. Cardinal Hippolytus d'Este, Archbishop of Milan,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brother of Alphonso , was the friend and patron of Ariosto. Hercules II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1534-58), son of Alphonso, was the husband of Renata, daughter of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Louis XII. of France, patroness of the Reformers Calvin and Marot, to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whom she accorded an asylum. Having declared herself in favour of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reformed doctrines, she was separated from her husband and children.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Her son Alphonso II. (1558-97) raised the glory of Ferrara to its culmin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ating point, but with him the family became extinct, his three marriages{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being childless. He was the patron of the poets Tasso and Guarini (author{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the 'Pastor Fido1, born at Ferrara in 1537, died at Venice in 1612).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Goethe in his 'Torquato Tasso1 has drawn a faithful picture of the court of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ferrara about the year 1575, although a somewhat ideal colouring is given{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to some of the characters. His description of the attachment of Tasso to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Eleonora (1537-81), the youngest unmarried sister of the duke, is however{% endraw %}
{% raw %}not without foundation. Anna (1531-1607), one of the sisters, was married{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Due de Guise, and afterwards to the Due de Nemours; Lucrezia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1534-98), the other sister, was the wife of the Duke of Urbino. Alphonso{% endraw %}
{% raw %}II. was succeeded by Cesare d'Este, descendant of a natural son of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alphonso I., but only as duke of Modena and Reggio, Ferrara and Com-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acchio having been claimed by Pope Clement VIII. as vacant fiefs. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}history of art and science the renown of the House of Este is immortal.{% endraw %}
