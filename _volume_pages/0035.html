{% raw %}In the 4th xentury the heathen world, which had long been in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a tottering condition, at length became Christianised, and a christian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}new period of art began. This is sometimes erroneously re- Period{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Roman art, and a sudden and spontaneous invention of a new style.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}But the eye and the hand adhere to custom more tenaciously than{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the mind. While new ideas, and altered views of the character of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Deity and the destination of man were entertained, the wonted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}forms were still necessarily employed in the expression of these{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thoughts. Moreover the heathen sovereigns had by no means been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unremittingly hostile to Christianity (the most bitter persecutions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}did not take place till the 3rd century), and the new doctrines were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}permitted to expand, take deeper root, and organise themselves in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the midst of heathen society. The consequence was, that the trans¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ition from heathen to Christian ideas of art was a gradual one, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that in point of form early Christian art continued to follow up the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tasks of the ancient. The best proof of this is afforded by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paintings of the Roman Catacombs. These were by no means ori¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ginally the secret, anxiously-concealed places of refuge of the prim¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}itive Christians, but constituted their legally-recognised, publicly-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accessible burial-places. Reared in the midst of the customs of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}heathen Rome, the Christian community perceived no necessity to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deviate from the artistic principles of antiquity. In the embellish¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment of the catacombs they adhered to the decorative forms handed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}down by their ancestors; and in design, choice of colour, grouping{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of figures, and treatment of subject, they were entirely guided by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the customary rules. Even the sarcophagus-sculptures of the 4th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and 5th centuries differ in purport only, and not in technical treat¬{% endraw %}
