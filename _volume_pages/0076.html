{% raw %}8 Route 1.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}LYONS.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From Paris{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Two magnificent new streets, the Rue de la Republique (PI. D,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}E, 3) and the Rue de VHotel-de-Ville (PL D, E. 3) lead from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}H6tel-de-Ville to the *Place Bellecour (formerly Louis le Grand ;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}PL E, 3), one of the most spacious squares in Europe, and adorned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with an Equestrian Statue of Louis XIV. by Lemot. On one side of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this square (No. 31) rises the Musee de la Propagation de la Foi,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}containing an extensive ethnographical collection formed of objects{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sent by the missionaries of the Propaganda from all parts of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}world (open daily, except Sun. and holidays, 8-5, on Frid. 10-5).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— The Rue de Bourbon leads thence to the Place Perrache with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the station of that name, abutting on the wide Cours du Midi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PI. F, 4), which is planted with rows of trees. Beyond the station,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and occupying the point of the tongue of land between the rivers,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the suburb Perrache, named after its founder (1770), and ra¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pidly increasing in extent.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Boulevard du Nord (PI. G, 2), on the left bank of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rhone, lies the handsome Musee Guimet, opened in 1878, contain¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing the collections formed by M. Emile Guimet during a journey{% endraw %}
{% raw %}undertaken under the auspices of the Ministry of Public Instruc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion to study the religions of the Orient. The contents of the mu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seum consist mainly of idols and other objects connected with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}religious rites of ancient and modern times, but also include spe¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cimens of the industrial arts and ethnographical curiosities. Several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the rooms contain paintings by M. Guimet's companion, M. Re-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gamey, illustrating the religious life of the East. The museum is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}open on Sun. from 11 to 5, and at other times on application to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the custodian (good catalogue).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vestibule. Roman works in marble. — Ground Floor. The first room{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contains Chinese porcelain; the others Japanese gods and legendary char¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acters, objects used in worship, grotesque articles, porcelain, fayence, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}First Floor. Library and Reading Room. — Room I. Objects from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}India, Anam, Thibet, Cambodia, and China. — Room II., Chinese articles.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— Rooms III-VI., Japanese articles. In the middle of the third room,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on a pedestal, is a Model of the Mandara of Koo-Boo-Daishi in the temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Too-dji (9th cent.), a sort of personification of the universe typified{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by a pantheon of the most eminent Buddhas. In the middle of Room VI.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is a wooden figure of Dharma, said to have been the first Buddhistic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}missionary in Japan (1st cent.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Second Floor. Rooms I. and II. contain early Egyptian statues, sar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cophagi, steles, mummies, amulets, and other antiquities. The walls of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Room I. are adorned with 12 scenes from the domestic life of the ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptians, copied from the mural paintings in the tomb of Ti at Sakkara,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}near Memphis (ca. 5000 B.C.). — In Rooms III. and IV. are Greek, Italic,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Gallic antiquities: Etruscan and Roman tombs; Pompeian mural{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paintings; examples of Greek and Roman workmanship in bronze, marble,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and terracotta; Gallic pottery and glass; Roman ornaments; Greek, Car¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thaginian, and Gallic coins, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Near the Musee Guimet, at the N. end of the town, lies the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*Parc de la Tete d'Or (PI. B, C, 1, 2; 1 M. from the Place des{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Terreaux), laid out in 1857, and containing rare plants, hothouses,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and pleasure-grounds in the style of the Bois de Boulogne at Paris.{% endraw %}
