{% raw %}Piacenza was founded by the Romans, B.C. 219, as Colonia Placentia,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the same time with Cremona. In the middle ages it held a high rank{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the league of the Lombard towns, and was afterwards frequently the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}subject of fierce party-struggles between the Scotti, Torriani, and Visconti.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In 1488 it was plundered by Francesco Sforza, a blow from which it never{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entirely recovered. In 1545 it finally came into the possession of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Farnese family and was united to Parma.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Piazza de' Cavalli (PL D, 3) is situated the *Palazzo del{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Comune (PL 12), erected at the end of the 13th century, and de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scribed by Burckhardt as 'one of the earliest instances of a worthy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and monumental embodiment in stone and lime of the growing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spirit of municipal independence'. On the ground-floor there is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a spacious arcade with five pointed arches; in the upper floor are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}six rich round-arch windows, above which rise handsome pinnacles.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In front of it stand the affected equestrian Statues of the Dukes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alessandro and Ranuccio Farnese, erected 1620-24, by Francesco{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mocchi, a pupil of Giovanni da Bologna. Alessandro attained great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distinction in the wars in the Netherlands as governor under Phi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lip II. He took Antwerp in 1585, besieged Paris in 1591, and died{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Arras in 1592. He was succeeded by his tyi annical son Ranuccio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(d. 1622).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Francesco (PL 4), a brick edifice in the Piazza, with Gothic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}interior, was erected in 1278. In front of it rises a statue to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Romagnosi (d. 1835), professor of constitutional law at Parma, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}editor of the new Italian penal code. — A little to the N.W. lies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Palazzo della Delegazione (PL 13; D,2, 3), now the prefettura.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The principal street (Via Diritta) leads to the E. to the * Cathe¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dral (PL 1; E, 3, 4), a Romanesque-Lombard edifice dating from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1122, with a superstructure of brick added in the 13th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the facade are there projecting porches with columns resting on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the backs of lion, above which are a circular window and open gal¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leries with dwarf pillars. In the interior, above the entrance, is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gothic reredos. The church contains admirable frescoes by Guercino{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(prophets and sibyls) on the dome, and by Lodovico Carracci on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the arch of the choir, and pictures by Procaccini (in the choir), and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Andrea and Elisabetta Sirani over the 3rd altar on the right.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The crypt is borne by 100 columns. — In the vicinity (take the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side-street to the left on leaving the cathedral) is —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Antonino (PL 3 ; D, E, 4), formerly the cathedral, dating from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}903, 1104, and 1562, with a fine old vestibule, called 'Paradiso'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1350), of curious irregular shape. The wide transept is near the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}W. end of the church, and from its intersection with the nave rises{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a tower borne by eight massive round columns. — Adjacent is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}handsome Theatre (PL 15), built in 1804.{% endraw %}
