{% raw %}VII. TUSCANY.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Etruscan Museum at Florence first affords us an opportunity of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}becoming acquainted with the artistic products of this ancient people in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bronze and earthenware, and obtaining an insight into their gloomy and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}realistic disposition. At Fiesole our attention will then be directed to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the huge stone structures erected by the Etruscans to defend their frontier{% endraw %}
{% raw %}against the predatory Ligurians of the Apennines. The connection{% endraw %}
{% raw %}between antiquity and modern times is not very apparent in this part of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the country, as the classic soil of Etruria lies somewhat to the S. of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}limits prescribed to the present Handbook. None of the twelve great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cities which divided among them the supremacy over the whole country{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lay on the Arno; and the beautiful valleys which now delight the eye of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the traveller, being exposed to the continual incursions of the Ligurians,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were marshy and desolate down to the 3rd cent. B. C, and did not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prosper till the time of the Romans. The history of the ancient Etrus¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cans may nevertheless appropriately be kept in view. If Florence forcibly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reminds the visitor at every step that modern Italy owes its noblest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aspirations and richest intellectual inheritance to this city and this land,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the student of history will be interested in remembering that the same{% endraw %}
{% raw %}office of disseminating civilisation among their compatriots was performed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Etruscans two thousand years before the modern development of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the country.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The power of the ancient Etruscans attained its zenith in the 6th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cent B.C.; but owing to the want of political coherence in their widely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ramified confederation, they were unable permanently to maintain their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}supremacy. As the whole of N. Italy had been conquered by the Celts,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Campania by the Samnites (in 424), so the Romans and Latins from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the lower Tiber gradually encroached on Etruria, and after protracted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}struggles wrested city after city from the confederation. In the 3rd cent.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the entire country thus became subject to the authority of Rome. By{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the establishment of numerous colonies, and abundant grants of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman citizenship, the country was gradually Latinised, and the Etruscan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}language', which has been handed down to us in several thousand still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}undeciphered inscriptions, was superseded by Latin. Some of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}peculiarities of the Tuscan dialect, such as the slight aspiration of the c{% endraw %}
{% raw %}before a (chasa for casa), are thought to be referable to the old language{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the country, but this is matter of mere conjecture. The traveller{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acquainted with Italian will have little difficulty in understanding the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}people of the country, as the modern written Italian language (lingua{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vulgaris, vulgare latinum, lingua toscana) is mainly derived from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dialects of Central Italy, and particularly that of Tuscany. This language{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is proved to have been used as early as the 10th cent, by the educated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}classes, as well as Latin, but Dante and the great Tuscan poets and prose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}writers were the first to give it grammatical regularity and precision. Though{% endraw %}
{% raw %}closely allied with the popular dialect, it is by no means identical with it.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}During the later imperial epoch the country formed the province of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tuscia, and was afterwards a Franconian county under the same name.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Pope Gregory VII., were dismembered after her death (1115), even before{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which municipal liberty had begun to spring up in the towns. Among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the rival communities Pisa, owing to its situation, attained the greatest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}maritime power, and like Milan, Venice., and Genoa, seemed destined to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}form the centre of a new state. In the 11th, 12th, and 13th centuries it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was by far the most important of the Tuscan cities, and while the citi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}zens were commemorating their victories by the erection of imposing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}buildings, Florence had hardly begun to exist. Florence was first indebted{% endraw %}
