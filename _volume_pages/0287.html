{% raw %}178 Route 28.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}PAVIA.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From Milan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pavia. — Crock Bianca (PL a; B. 4), B^ 4, D. 5, B. 272, S. 4-,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}L. 1, omnibus 1 fr.; Lombardia (PL b; B, 3); Tre Re (PI. c; B, 5). —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cafi Demetrio, Corso Vittorio Emmanuele.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cab per drive 80 c, per hour 172fr. — Omnibus to the town 25 c{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The names of many of the streets have been altered recently; the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}old names are generally given in red lettering below the new.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pavia, with 30,000 inhab., capital of the province of the same{% endraw %}
{% raw %}name, situated near the confluence of the Ticino and the Po, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ticinum of the ancients, subsequently Papla, was also known as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Citth di Cento Torri from its numerous towers, of which only a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}few still exist. In the middle ages it was the faithful ally of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}German emperors, until it was subjugated by the Milanese, and it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is still partly surrounded by the walls and fortifications of that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period. A visit to the town requires about 3 hours.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Leaving the railway-station, we enter the Corso Cavour (PL{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A, 4) through the Porta Borgorato or Marengo (in a wall to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right is the statue of a Roman magistrate), and following the Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Jacopo Brossolaro to the right reach the Piazza del Duomo.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Cathedral (PL 4; B, 4), rising on the site of an ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}basilica, begun in accordance with a design by Bramante, and con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tinued by Cristoforo Rocchi in 1486, but never completed, is a vast{% endraw %}
{% raw %}circular structure with four arms.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Interior, on the right, is the sumptuous "Area di S. Agostino,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adorned with 290 figures (of saints, and allegorical), begun, it is supposed,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1362 by Bonino da Campiglione, by whom the figures on the tombs of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Scaliger family at Verona (p. 201) were executed. To the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the entrance is a wooden model of the church as originally projected.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The gateway to the left of the church is in the late-Romanesque{% endraw %}
{% raw %}style. Adjoining it rises a massive Campanile, begun in 1583.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We may now proceed to the Corso Vittorio Emmanuele, a street{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intersecting the town in a straight direction from N. to S., from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Porta di Milano to the Porta Ticinese , and leading to the covered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bridge (14th cent.; a pleasant promenade with picturesque view){% endraw %}
{% raw %}over the Ticino, which is here navigated by barges and steamboats.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A chapel stands on the bridge, halfway across.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Michele (PL 7; B, 5), to which the third side-street to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right leads (coming from the bridge), a Romanesque church errone¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ously ascribed to the Lombard kings, belongs to the latter part of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}11th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The facade is adorned with numerous very ancient reliefs in sand¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stone, in ribbon-like stripes, and a curious gabled gallery. The nave and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aisles are supported by eight pillars, from which rise double round arches.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The short choir, under which there is a crypt, terminates in an apse.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Over the centre of the transept rises a dome. The pillars of the nave{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bear traces of ancient frescoes. The interior has lately been restored.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The traveller may now ascend the Corso Vitt. Emmanuele to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}University (PL 31 ; B, 4), founded in 1361 on the site of a school{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of law, which had existed here since the 10th century. The build¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing is much handsomer than that of Padua; the quadrangles of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}interior are surrounded by handsome arcades and embellished with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous memorial-tablets, busts, and monuments of celebrated{% endraw %}
