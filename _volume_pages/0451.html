{% raw %}S. Pietro, in the 'baroque' style, begun in 1605. It consists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a spacious nave with circular vaulting, the aisles being partly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}converted into chapels with lofty galleries, and partly into separate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apartments. In the chapter-room, St. Peter with the Madonna;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above the choir an Annunciation, the last work of Lod. Carracci.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— Adjoining it on the N.E. is the Palazzo Arcivescovile (PL 28),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a court constructed by Tibaldi in 1577. — In the vicinity, to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the N.W. of S. Pietro, is the small church of the Madonna di Gal-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}liera (PL 13 ; D, E, 4), with an interesting, but dilapidated facade{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of 1470. — Opposite is the Pal. Fava (PL 41; D, 4), with fine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*Frescoes by the Carracci from the myths of Jason and iEneas.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We now return to the Via Rizzoli, at the E. end of which,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nearly in the centre of the town, are the Leaning Towers (PL F, 4),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the most singular structures in Bologna. The Torre Asinelli (PL 32),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected in 1109 by Gherardo degli Asinelli, which looks prodigiously{% endraw %}
{% raw %}high when seen from the pavement below, is 320 ft. in height and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4 ft. out of the perpendicular. A rough staircase of 447 steps leads{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the summit, which commands a fine view. The Torre Garisenda{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL 33), erected in 1110 by Filippo and Ottone Garisenda, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}163 ft. high only, but is 10 ft. out of the perpendicular. Dante (In¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ferno xxxi. 136) compares the giant Antaeus, who bends towards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}him, to this tower, 'when a cloud passes over it'. The latter is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}probably the only one of the many leaning towers in Italy whose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obliquity has been intentional (comp. p. 345), but it was found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}impossible to complete it.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Piazza di Porta Ravegnana , in front of the leaning{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towers, stands the handsome Guildhouse of the Stracciatori ('Uni-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}versitas Interpolatorum'), said to have been built by Franc. Francia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1496 and restored in 1620.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the leaning towers five streets radiate to the gates of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}same names: the Via Castiglione, S. Stefano, Mazzini, S. Vitale,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Zamboni. To the right at the corner of the Via S. Stefano{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Via Castiglione is situated the *PaIazzo della Mercanzia (PL{% endraw %}
{% raw %}F, 5), or Foro de' Mercanti (Chamber of Commerce), a Gothic struc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ture, said to have been erected in 1294, and restored by the Benti¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vogli in 1493. The interior is adorned with the armorial bearings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of all the jurists who taught law here from 1441 to 1800. — Farther{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. in the Via Castiglione is the Pal. Pepoli (PL 51; F, 5), of 1344,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the castellated residence of this once powerful family, with a rich{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gateway and an imposing court with a colonnade on one side and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arched passages on the three others. — Farther on, to the right,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rises the handsome new Cassa di Risparmio (PL 25; E, 5), with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arcades on the ground-floor, and handsome wrought-iron gratings at{% endraw %}
