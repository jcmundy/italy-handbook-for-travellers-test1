{% raw %}374 Route 53. FLORENCE. History.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The pioneers of painting in the Renaissance period were Paolo Uccelli{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1397-1475), and Masaccio (1401-28), whose immediate successors were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fiiippo Lippi, the monk (1412-69), his son Filippino Lippi (1457-1504), and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alessandro Botticelli (1447-1510). The chief aims of the school were to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}master the technical intricacies of the art, to invest each figure with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beauty, to arrange the groups harmoniously, and to cultivate a faithful{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portraiture of real life. The most famous representative of the school{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was Domenico Ghirlandajo (1449-94), whose chief rivals were Cosimo Ros-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}selli (1430-1507), Antonio and Piero Pollajuolo (1441-89?), and Andrea Ver-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rocchio (1435-88). In fervency of religious sentiment Fra Angelico da{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fiesole (1387-1455), by whom Benozzo Gozzoli was afterwards influenced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 343), stands pre-eminent. The history of Leonardo da Vinci, Michael{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Angelo Buonarroti, and Raphael, the princes of Italian art, is not per¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manently associated with Florence, but their residence in this city exer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cised a material influence on their respective careers. Leonardo and Michael{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Angelo may be regarded as belonging to Florence owing to the completion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of their studies there, and it was at Florence that Raphael supplemented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his art education, and shook off the trammels of the Umbrian school.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About 1506 the art history of Florence attained its most glorious period.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Leonardo, Michael Angelo, and Raphael were then engaged here together,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and with them were associated Lorenzo di Credi (1459-1537), a master{% endraw %}
{% raw %}closely allied to Leonardo, Fra Bartolommeo (1475-1517), an intimate friend{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Raphael, and the talented colourist Andrea del Sarto (1487-1531), while{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the two last were rivalled by Albertinelli, Franciabigio, Pontormo, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ridolfo Ghirlandajo. The union of the greatest masters at Rome, effected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Julius II. and Leo X., at length detracted from the reputation of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Florence, and the despotic sway of the Medici tended to check farther{% endraw %}
{% raw %}development. After the middle of the 16th cent. Florence produced no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}architecture worthy of note; among the sculptors the vulgar Baccio Ban-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dinelli (1493-1560) may be mentioned solely on account of his failures as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an imitator of Michael Angelo; and the province of painting, although{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cultivated with more success, now proved destitute of depth and in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dependence. Florence was the chief headquarters of the mannerist imitators{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Michael Angelo, the most eminent of whom were Giorgio Vasari, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}well known biographer of artists (1511-74), Angelo Bronzino, and Alessandro{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Allori. In the 17th cent, the principal Florentine artists were Luigi Cardi,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surnamed Cigoli, Cristofano Allori (1577-1621), Furini, and the somewhat{% endraw %}
{% raw %}insipid Carlo Dolci (1616-86). — The most distinguished sculptors of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}present century are Dupri (d. 1879), Fantacchiotti, and Zocchi, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most eminent painters are Stefano Ussi and Cassioli.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Florence is situated on both banks of the Arno, but by far{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the greater part of the city lies on the right bank. On the latter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the N. of the Ponte Vecchio, at some distance from the river,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was situated the Roman town of Florentia, which however was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extended at an early period in the middle ages to the opposite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bank of the Arno. The walls of the city, which have recently been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}almost entirely removed, were constructed at the same time as the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cathedral, between 1285 and 1388. The ancient Gates however{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have been spared, of which the following are the most interesting:{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Porta alia Croce (PL I, 6), erected in 1284, with frescoes by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ghirlandajo; Porta S. Gallo (PI. H, I, 1), erected in 1330, once also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adorned with frescoes by Ghirlandajo; Porta Romana (PI. A, 7),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected in 1328 by Jacopo Orcagna; Porta S. Frediano (PI. B, 4);{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Porta S. Miniato (PL F, 8). The New Quarters of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}town are at the W. end, on the right bank of the Arno, extending{% endraw %}
