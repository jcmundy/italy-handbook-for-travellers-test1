{% raw %}and sculptures are built into the walls, such as a good female head, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sarcophagus of a child, with interesting representations, perhaps Christian.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Among the Manuscripts, is the celebrated MS. of Aristophanes of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}10th cent., one of Dante of 1369, another by Pietro Dante; letters of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cicero of the 15th cent.; commentary of Benvenuto da Imola; prayer-book{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Mary Stuart with miniatures; visitors^ book from the tomb of Dante{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 328); the wooden coffin which contained the remains of the great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}poet, found in 1865 in the course of researches instituted on the 6th cen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tenary of his birth; rare editions, such as the Decretals of Boniface VIII.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}printed by Fust at Strassburg in 1465, and a number of ieditiones prin-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cipes\ There is also a small collection of objects in ivory, bronzes, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coins of the popes, the Medici, and the Malatesta, the golden ornaments of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the helmet of King Odoacer (?), etc. (all shown gratuitously).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The old Refectory of the Camaldulensians (shown by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}custodian, or by the sacristan of St. Romuald, door to the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}opposite the refectory) contains the Marriage at Cana in fresco, by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Luca and Francesco Longhi, and some fine carving on the door.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— The altars of the monastery-church of S. Romualdo are richly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}decorated with rare and beautiful marbles; in the 2nd chapel on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the left St. Romuald by Guercino; 3rd chapel on the left, a beau¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tiful ciborium (with candelabrum and cross from the sacristy) in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lapis lazuli, frescoes by Longhi.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Niccolo (PL 21; D, 6), built by Archb. Sergius in 760{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(closed), contains numerous paintings by the Augustinian monk{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Padre Cesare Pronti and by Francesco da Cotignola.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Agata (PL 2; D, 6; entrance in the Via Mazzini, between{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nos. 96 and 97), of the 5th cent., consisting of nave and aisles{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a vestibule, contains fine columns of marble. No. 295 in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}same street, not far from the Piazza S. Francesco, was once occu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pied by Lord Byron (p. 325), as the memorial tablet records.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Francesco (PL 12; D, 5) is said to have been founded by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}St. Petrus Chrysologus about the year 450, on the site of a temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Neptune, but is now entirely modernised.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Interior consists of nave and aisles, with 22 columns of coloured{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marble. Unpleasing modern ceiling. At the entrance are several ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tombstones; on the right that of Ostasio da Polenta of 1396; on the left{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that of Enrico Alfieri, who died in 1405 as general of the Franciscans.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the right the sarcophagus of the archbishop St. Liberius, of the 5th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}century. The Cappella del Crocefisso, the 2nd on the right, contains two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}"Columns of Greek marble with capitals and ornamentation by Pietro{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lombardi.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}poet died at Ravenna, where he enjoyed the protection of Guido da{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Polenta, on 14th Sept., 1321, at the age of 56, and was interred{% endraw %}
