{% raw %}Lucca (Roman Luca) was founded at a very remote period. It{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first belonged to Liguria, afterwards to Etruria, and became an im¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portant municipium. In B.C. 56, Julius Caesar, who was then governor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Gaul, held a conference here with Pompey and Crassus, with whom{% endraw %}
{% raw %}he had been associated since B. C. 60, in order to discuss a plan for the ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ministration of the Roman empire for the ensuing five years. The splen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dour of Lucca at that period is still indicated by the remains of the Ro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}man "Amphitheatre near S. Frediano. After the fall of the Roman Empire{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lucca belonged successively to the Goths, Lombards, and Franks, then{% endraw %}
{% raw %}became a duchy, and in the 12th cent, a republic. The feuds of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Guelphs and Ghibellines impaired the strength of the place so seriously{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that in 1314 it was compelled to succumb to Ugucione della Faggiuola{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Arezzo, the warlike governor of Pisa. Dante resided with his friend{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Uguccione at Lucca in 1314, and there became enamoured of the youth¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ful Gentucca (Purgatorio xxiv. 23), but he does not describe the inhabitants{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in very flattering terms (Inferno xxi. 41). After the expulsion of Uguc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cione , Lucca fell in 1325 into the hands of the powerful Castruccio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Castrani degli Interminelli of Lucca, who was also master of Pisa and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pistoja. On 23rd Sept. 1325 he defeated the Florentines at Altopascio, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1327 was nominated imperial governor of Tuscany by Emp. Lewis the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bavarian. On his death in 1328 the power of Lucca declined; its next{% endraw %}
{% raw %}master was Martino della Scala; it subsequently came into the possession{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Pisa, but in 1369 purchased its own freedom from Charles IV. for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}300,000 fl., and remained independent till the invasion of the French in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1799. In 1805 Napoleon gave Lucca as- a principality to his sister Elisa{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bacciocchi; in 1814 it came into the possession of the dukes of Parma{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the house of Bourbon, who in 1847 ceded it to Tuscany.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the History of Medieval Architecture, Lucca, like Pisa, occu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pied an important position at a very early period. The churches of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Frediano and S. Michele were both founded upwards of a thousand{% endraw %}
{% raw %}years ago, though probably little now remains of the original edifices.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The columns in S. Frediano, like those of the early Christian basilicas{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Rome, are antique. The taste for building, probably stimulated by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rivalry with Pisa, was again revived in the 12th cent., when the older{% endraw %}
{% raw %}churches were altered and restored, doubtless in accordance with Pisan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}models. — Towards the end of the 15th cent., Matteo Civitali (1435-1501),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one of the most pleasing sculptors of the early Renaissance, resided,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and produced numerous works, at Lucca. His style somewhat resembles{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the best pictures of that period, and, though full of life, is of a graceful{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and gentle character, contrasting especially with Donatello. — The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pictures of Fra Bartolommeo in the cathedral and S. Romano (the latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now removed to the Palazzo Pubblico) are also worthy of notice.{% endraw %}
