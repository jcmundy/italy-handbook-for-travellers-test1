{% raw %}The **Pont du Gard, a bridge and aqueduct over the Gard, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}descends from the Cevennes, passing the town of Alais with its extensive{% endraw %}
{% raw %}iron-works, is one of the grandest Roman works in existence. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desolate rocky valley of the Gard is bridged over by a threefold series{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of arches (the lowest 6, the next 11, and the highest 35 in number) which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}present a most majestic appearance. Agrippa, the general of Augustus,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is generally supposed to have been the founder, but without satisfactory{% endraw %}
{% raw %}evidence. The object of this structure was to supply Nimes with water{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the springs of Airan near St. Quentin and Ure near Uzes, a distance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of 25 M. Several arches are also seen to the N. of the Pont du Gard, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other traces of the aqueduct still exist nearer the town. The bridge for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carriages was added to the Roman aqueduct in 1743.{% endraw %}
