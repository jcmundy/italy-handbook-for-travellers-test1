{% raw %}The Lago di Garda (226 ft.), the Lacus Benacus of the Romans,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the largest of the N. Italian lakes, is 37 M. in length , and ly^-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}10 M. broad; area 189 sq. M., depth in many places upwards of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1000 ft. The whole lake belongs to Italy, except the N. extremity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with Riva, which is Austrian.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The lake is rarely perfectly calm, and in stormy weather is almost as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rough as the sea, a circumstance recorded by Virgil (Georg. ii. 160). The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}blue water, like that of all the Alpine lakes, is remarkably clear. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carpione, or salmon-trout, which attains a weight of 25 lbs., the trutta, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trout, 1-172 lb., the lagone, and the sardene are excellent fish.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The banks, although inferior in attraction to those of the Lake of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Como, present a great variety of beautiful landscapes, enhanced by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}imposing expanse of the water. The shores of the S. half are flat and well{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cultivated, but they become bolder between Capo S. Vigilio and a point{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the N. of Said, where the lake contracts. The vegetation is luxuriant,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}especially on the more sheltered W. bank. Even the sensitive lemon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arrives at maturity here, but the trees require to be carefully covered in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}winter. This is accomplished with the aid of numerous white pillars of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brick, 8-20 ft. in height, erected at regular intervals, and united by trans¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}verse beams at the top. The fruit is more bitter and aromatic than that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Sicily, suffers less from carriage, and keeps longer. Price in plentiful{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seasons 3-4 fr. per hundred, but frequently as high as 10 fr.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Desenzano (Mayer's Hotel, prettily situated and well spoken of;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Posta Vecchia; *Due Colombe, moderate), a small town with 4300{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inhab., at the S.W. angle of the lake, is a railway-station (p. 185).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Omn. from the steamboat to the train 50 c, luggage 25 c.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the E., not quite half-way to Peschiera (p. 185), is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}narrow promontory of Sermione, projecting 3 M. into the lake,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which here attains its greatest breadth.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A pleasant excursion may be made thither by boat or by carriage{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(6 M. from Desenzano), but the road is not recommended to walkers.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The fishing-village (poor locanda) adjoins the handsome ruin of a castle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Scaligers (p. 200). "We then cross the olive-clad height, past the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}little church of S. Pietro, to (1 M.) the extremity of the peninsula,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where we obtain a charming view. On the hill are remains of baths,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and on the promontory are relics of a building extending out into the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lake, which are said to have belonged to the country house of Catullus,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who wrote his poems here ('Sirmio peninsularum insularumque ocellus1).{% endraw %}
