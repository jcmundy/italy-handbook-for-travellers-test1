{% raw %}Romans therefore preferred to combine them with the arch-prin¬{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The individuality of the Greek orders, and their originally un¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}alterable coherence were thereby sacrificed, and divested of much{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of their importance; that which once possessed a definite organic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}significance frequently assumed a superficial and decorative charac¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ter ; but the aggregate effect is always imposing, the skill in blend¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing contrasts, and the directing taste admirable. The lofty gravity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Doric Style j must not be sought for at Rome. The Doric{% endraw %}
{% raw %}t Those unacquainted with architecture will easily learn to distinguish{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the different Greek styles. In the Doric the shafts of the columns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(without bases) rest immediately on the common pavement, in the Ionic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they are separated from it by bases. The flutings of the Doric column{% endraw %}
{% raw %}immediately adjoin each other, being separated by a sharp ridge, while{% endraw %}
{% raw %}those of the Ionic are disposed in pairs, separated by broad unfluted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intervening spaces. The Doric capital, expanding towards the summit,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}somewhat resembles a crown of leaves, and was in fact originally adorned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with painted representations of wreaths; the Ionic capital is distinguished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the volutes (or scrolls) projecting on either side, which may be re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}garded rather as an appropriate covering of the capital than as the cap¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ital itself. The entablature over the columns begins in the Doric style{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the simple, in the Ionic with the threefold architrave; above which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the Doric order are the metopes (originally openings, subsequently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}receding panels) and triglyphs (tablets with two angular grooves in front,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and a half groove at each end, resembling extremities of beams), and in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Ionic the frieze with its sculptured enrichments. In the temples of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}both orders the front culminates in a pediment. The so-called Tuscan,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or early Italian column, approaching most nearly to the Doric, exhibits{% endraw %}
{% raw %}no decided distinctive marks; the Corinthian, with the rich capital{% endraw %}
{% raw %}formed of acanthus-leaves, is essentially of a decorative character only.{% endraw %}
