{% raw %}In the History of Art Venice has shown herself as independent of,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and distinct from the mainland, as are her situation and her political{% endraw %}
{% raw %}history. The sensation of novelty experienced by the traveller who visits{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Venice for the first time, even after having seen the whole of the rest of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Italy, will also be felt by those who begin to study her art. The earliest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monuments of Venice at once betray the fact that her greatness was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}founded on her Oriental commerce. The church of St. Mark is in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Byzantine style, the oldest mosaics bear a Byzantine impress, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}same type is observable in other branches of art. Even during the period{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Gothic Art the Venetians did not adopt the same forms as the rest of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Italy. In the building of their churches several architects from the main¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}land (including perhaps Niccolb Pisano) appear to have been summoned to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their aid. Their palaces, which, like those of Upper Italy, generally form{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the chief examples of Gothic, particularly that of the Doges, exhibit{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a very peculiar character. They usually possess a large entrance colon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nade, a loggia on the upper floor with a number of windows close together{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the middle, wings, treated rather as spaces for the reception of paint¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ings , and everywhere abundance of decoration and colour. Examples of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this style are the Cct d'Oro, and the Palazzo Foscari. At a later period the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Renaissance Architecture, which did not become naturalised till the end{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the 15th cent., was still more zealously cultivated. In point of size{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the early-Renaissance buildings at Venice bear no comparison with those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Tuscany, but are more richly decorated, and the palaces retain the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}articulation peculiar to the earliest period. At a later date, when art{% endraw %}
{% raw %}began to decline, the Venetian architecture resisted the influences of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bad taste longer than that of Central Italy. Among the most important{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Venetian architects were several members of the Lombardi family,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Jacopo Sansovino of Florence (1477-1570), Antonio da Ponte, and lastly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Andrea Palladia of Vicenza (1518-1580), who inaugurated a new era,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}especially in church-architecture, by limiting the facade to a single range{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of massive columns. Palladio's chief successors were Scamozzi and Longhena.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the province of Sculpture the master who designed the statuary on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Palace of the Doges (perhaps Fil. Calendario) was the most famous of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the middle ages. About the middle of the 15th cent, the growing taste{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for monumental tombs afforded abundant employment to the sculptors, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the studios which now sprung up issued the numerous magnificent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monuments which still fill the churches of Venice. The names of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bregni or Rizzi, of the Lombardi (probably not natives), and of Alessandro{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Leopardo, are the most important. At a later period Jacopo Sansovino{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was the leading master here, both as a sculptor and an architect.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}His works, though often designed chiefly for pictorial effect, are far more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pleasing than those of Michael Angelo's school. His pupils were Girolamo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Campagna and Alessandro Vittoria (d. 1607).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Venetian Painters did not begin to attract universal attention{% endraw %}
{% raw %}till the beginning of the 16th century. In the 14th cent, they were far in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ferior to those of the other Italian schools, and though Giotto was engaged{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for a considerable time at the neighbouring Padua, they were unaffected{% endraw %}
