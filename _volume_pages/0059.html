{% raw %}ITALIAN ART.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Whether the palm be due to Michael Angelo or to Raphael (1483-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1520) among the artists of Italy is a question which formerly gave{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rise to vehement discussion among artists and amateurs. Raphael{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The admirer of Michael Angelo need, however, by no means{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be precluded from enjoying the works of Raphael. We now know{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that it is far more advantageous to form an acquaintance with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}each master in his peculiar province, than anxiously to weigh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their respective merits ; and the more minutely we examine their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}works, the more firmly we are persuaded that neither in any way{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obstructed the progress of the other, and that a so-called higher{% endraw %}
{% raw %}combination of the two styles was impossible. Michael Angelo's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unique position among his contemporaries was such, that no one,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Raphael not excepted, was entirely exempt from his influence;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but the result of preceding development was turned to the best{% endraw %}
{% raw %}account, not by him, but by Raphael, whose susceptible and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}discriminating character enabled him at once to combine different{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tendencies within himself, and to avoid the faults of his pre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}decessors. Raphael's pictures are replete with indications of pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}found sentiment, but his imagination was so constituted that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}he did not distort the ideas which he had to embody in order{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to accommodate them to his own views, but rather strove to iden¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tify himself with them, and to reproduce them with the utmost{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fidelity. In the case of Raphael, therefore, a knowledge of his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}works and the enjoyment of them are almost inseparable, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it is difficult to point out any single sphere with which he was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}especially familiar. He presents to us with equal enthusiasm{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pictures of the Madonna, and the myth of Cupid and Psyche; in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gieat cyclic compositions he is as brilliant as in the limited sphere{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of portrait-painting; at one time he appears to attach paramount{% endraw %}
{% raw %}importance to strictness of style, architectural arrangement, sym¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}metry of groups, etc. ; at other times one is tempted to believe that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}he regarded colour as his most effective auxiliary. His excellence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}consists in his rendering equal justice to the most varied subjects,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and in each case as unhesitatingly pursuing the right course, both{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in his apprehension of the idea and selection of form, as if he had{% endraw %}
{% raw %}never followed any other.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Little is known of Raphael's private life, nor is it known by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}what master he was trained after his father's death (1494). In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1500 he entered the studio of Perugino (p. li), and probably soon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}assisted in the execution of some of the works of his prolific master.{% endraw %}
