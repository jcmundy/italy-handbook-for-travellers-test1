{% raw %}In the province of Painting the first master who attained more than{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a local reputation was Francesco Francia (1450-1517), the goldsmith, a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pupil of Zoppo of Ferrara. In the devotion and gracefulness of his female{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figures he almost rivals Perugino. His son Giacomo Francia was influenced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Venetian school, while at the same time the school of Raphael{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gained ground at Bologna. The chief adherents of the latter were Bartol.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ramenghi, surnamed Bagnacavallo (d. 1542), and Innocenzo da Imbla (d.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1550?). Bologna attained its greatest importance at the close of the 16th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}century. The mannerism into which Italian painting had gradually lapsed,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was resisted by the Eclectics, whose style was mainly introduced by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lodovico Carracci (1555-1619). In teaching at his academy he inculcated a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thorough mastery of the elements of art, a comprehensive education, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a careful study of the great masters. The school was afterwards carried{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on by his cousins Agostino (1558-1601) and Annibale Carracci (1560-1609),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the last of whom in particular possessed a refined sense of colour, devel¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}oped by the study of Correggio. To this school belonged also Guido{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Reni (1574-1642), Domenichino (Domenico Zampieri; 1581-1641), and Albani{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1578-1660), who exercised a great influence on Italian art in the 17th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cent., and. effected a temporary revival of good taste. They afterwards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}came into collision with the naturalists, chiefly at Rome and Naples, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Bologna their sway was undisputed.{% endraw %}
