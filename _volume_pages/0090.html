{% raw %}Massilia was a colony founded about B.C. 600 by Greeks from Phocsea{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Asia Minor, who soon became masters of the sea, defeated the Car¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thaginians in a naval battle near Corsica, and stood in friendly alliance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the Romans as early as B. C. 390. They also established new colonies in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their neighbourhood, such as Tauroeis (near Ciotat), Olbia (near Hyeres),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Antipolis (Antibes), and Nicaea (Nice), all of which, like their founders{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adhered to the Greek language, customs, and culture. Massilia maintained{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this reputation until the imperial period of Rome, and was therefore treat{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed with leniency and respect by Julius Caesar when conquered by him,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}B.C. 49. Tacitus informs us that his father-in-law Agricola, a native of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the neighbouring Roman colony of Forum Julii (Frejus), found, even under{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Claudius, ample opportunities for completing his education at Massilia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the Greek manner, for which purpose Athens was usually frequented.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The town possessed temples of Diana (on the site of the present cathedral),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Neptune (on the coast), of Apollo, and other gods. Its government was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aristocratic. After the fall of the W. Empire Marseilles fell successively{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into the hands of the Visigoths, the Franks, and Arelate; it was de¬{% endraw %}

{% raw %}the Vicomtes de Marseille; in 1218 it became independent, but shortly af¬{% endraw %}
