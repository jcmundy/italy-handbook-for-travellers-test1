{% raw %}The Small Antiquities are on the First Floor, and consist of mum¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mies, papyrus writings, scarabees, trinkets, vases, and porcelain statu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ettes and terracottas, many of which are Graeco-Roman. In the centre of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the second room is the formerly celebrated Tabula Isiaca, found in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pontificate of Pope Paul III. (d. 1549) in the Villa Caffarelli at Rome, a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tablet of bronze with hieroglyphics and figures partially inlaid with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}silver. Attempts to decipher the characters elicited the most profound{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and erudite explanations and conjectures from the savants of three cen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}turies, but it has been recently proved that the tablet is spurious, having{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been manufactured at Rome in the reign of Hadrian. The celebrated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}papyrus with fragments of the annals of Manetho (a list of the kings of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt down to the 19th dynasty), discovered by Champollion, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Book of the Dead', edited by Lepsius, are also preserved here. — We{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now turn to the left into a room containing antiquities from Cyprus, at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the door of which are two Assyrian reliefs, the heads of a king and a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}eunuch.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beyond, on the left, is a room devoted to Roman Sculptures: in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the middle, heads of poets and philosophers; along the window-wall,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}busts of emperors; in the corner to the left, colossal head of a goddess,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}found at Alba in 1839, fine *Head of Venus (bust modern), head of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Antinous, etc. On the right are the Grseco-Etruscan Vases and Terra¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cottas ('Head of Medusa, Mercury and a youth, Olympus from the group{% endraw %}
{% raw %}already mentioned, graceful dancing nymphs; by the window, early Ital¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ian vessels), and the Bronzes, including a tripod and a *Silenus , found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}near Turin, head of Caligula, and *Minerva, found in the Versa near{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Stradella in 1829. A few silver reliefs are also exhibited here. — The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}room in the middle contains terracottas and glass.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Picture Gallery (Pinacoteca), on the second floor, con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sists of 15 rooms containing upwards of 500 paintings. This col¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lection , being of recent date , cannot boast of a very distinct{% endraw %}
{% raw %}character like most of the other Italian galleries; but it affords the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller an excellent opportunity of becoming better acquainted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the works of Gaudenzio Ferrari (1484-1549), in which we{% endraw %}
{% raw %}can distinctly trace Leonardo's inspiration, coupled with the in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fluence of the Umbrian school (Nos. 49 and 54). The early Pied-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}montese painter, Macrino d'Alba (1460-1510), and his pupil De-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ferrari da Chivasso may also be studied here. Sodoma (Giovanni{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Antonio Bazzi, 1480-1549), who originally belonged to the Lom¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bard school, is well represented by three pictures. Lorenzo di{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Credi's (1459-1537) Madonna, No. 356, of this master's best period,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shows that he was influenced by Leonardo. Numerous and impor¬{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Cristus; 358. Memling; 340. Sketch by Rubens; 338, 351, 363,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}384. by Van Dyck. (Catalogue 1 fr. 25 c).{% endraw %}
