{% raw %}Vicenza, the Vicetia of the ancients, the capital of a province,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with 27,000 inhab. (commune 39,500), lies at the N. base of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Monti Berici (see above), on both sides of the Bacchiglione, near its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}confluence with the Retrone. Though the houses for the most part{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are crowded, the town possesses many interesting palaces, to which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}half-a-day may profitably be devoted.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vicenza, like all larger towns of N. Italy, boasted in the 15th cent.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a School of Painting, which, though it was influenced by Mantegna,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and never produced masters of the highest rank, yielded results of consid¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erable importance. The earliest master of note was Giovanni Speranza,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who, however, was soon surpassed by Bartolommeo Montagna (who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}flourished here in 1484-1523). The gallery and the churches (the Cathe¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dral, S. Corona, and S. Lorenzo) of Vicenza contain works by the latter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and he is represented at Padua and Verona also. His compositions are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}strongly realistic, and he shows a predilection for muscular figures, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for colouring of a rich brownish tint. His drapery is ungraceful, but,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}like that of Diirer, boldly defined. His son, Benedetto Montagna, was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unimportant, but his contemporary Giovanni Buonconsiglio (d. 1530), a fol¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lower of Antonello da Messina, has produced some pleasing works. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 16th cent. Vicenza lost its importance in the history of painting, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}attained a high reputation in the province of Architecture, having given{% endraw %}
{% raw %}birth to Andrea Palladio (1518-1560), the last great architect of the Re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}naissance, the chief sphere of whose operations was his native town. By{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his study of the antique in Rome he was enabled to effect a revival of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}what may be termed the ancient language of forms, and he made it his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}endeavour to exhibit in his buildings the organic connection between the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}different members. The chief characteristic of his school consists in a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}studious adherence to impressive simplicity of form, and a very sparing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}indulgence in the lavish enrichments in which the early-Renaissance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was too apt to revel. His finest churches are at Venice, but his most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous palaces are at Vicenza, to which they impart a uniform and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}handsome appearance.{% endraw %}
