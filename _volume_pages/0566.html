{% raw %}In the Second Court (closed in 1882; comp. p. 416) stands the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}celebrated :f:David ('II Gigante') by Michael Angelo, shaped by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}youthful artist in 1501 -1504 from a gigantic block of marble,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which, had been abandoned as spoiled. The statue, which is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}placed under a glass building covered with a dome, formerly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stood in front of the Palazzo Vecchio (p. 375).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'No plastic work of Michael Angelo earned such a harvest of laudation{% endraw %}
{% raw %}among his contemporaries as the 'David'. Vasari sings the praises of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}miracle-worker, who raised the dead, spoiled block to new life, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}assures us that Michael Angelo's David is vastly superior to all ancient and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}modern statues whatever. The boldness and assured touch of the great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sculptor certainly awake our admiring astonishment. Not only the sub¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ject was prescribed to him, but also its size and proportions, added to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which he was confined to the narrowest limits for the development of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}attitude and motion. Yet this constraint is not perceptible, and the history{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the statue could by no means be divined from its appearance. Out¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wardly the demeanour of the young hero is composed and quiet; but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}each limb is animated by a common impulse from within, and the whole{% endraw %}
{% raw %}body is braced up for one action. The raised left arm holds the sling in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}readiness, the right hand hanging at his side conceals the pebble; next{% endraw %}
{% raw %}instant he will make the attack'. — Springer.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This court is also destined for the reception of the 'Michael{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Angelo Museum', in which a complete collection of casts and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}photographs of the great master's works is to be exhibited.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The same building (entrance in the Via Alfani, No. 82){% endraw %}
{% raw %}contains the celebrated manufactory of Florentine Mosaics (a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}branch of industry founded in the middle of the 16th cent.),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}containing a collection of the materials used and of finished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}works, open daily, 10-4 (adm. 50c, Sun. free).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The custodian of the Academy also keeps the keys of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cloisters of the Recollets, or barefooted monks (Chiostro della{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Compagnia dello Scalzo), Via Cavour 69 (PL G. 2), with an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}elegant court of the 16th cent., adorned with admirable *Frescoes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in grisaille from the history of John the Baptist, with allegorical{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figures and rich ornamentation, executed by Andrea del Sarto,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the assistance of Franciabigio.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1. Allegorical figure of Faith (1520); 2. The Angel appearing to Za-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}charias (1526); 3. Visitation (1524); 4. Nativity of the Baptist (1526);{% endraw %}
{% raw %}5. Departure of John from his father's house, and 6. His meeting with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christ (these two by Franciabigio, 1518-19). — 7. Baptism of Christ (the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}earliest and weakest of all, perhaps painted by the two artists in com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mon, 1509); 8. Allegorical figure of Love (1520); 9. Allegorical figure of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Justice (1515); 10. John preaching in the desert (1515); 11. John baptising{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1517); 12. Joh,n made prisoner (1517); 13. Dance of Salome; 14. Death of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}John; 15. His head brought in on a charger; 16. Allegorical figure of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hope (the last four painted in 1523). — It is interesting to remark in several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of these frescoes the influence of Albert Diirer, whose downright truth¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fulness early impressed the Italian artists (Raphael among them), in spite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the contrast between them and him in form and conception. For{% endraw %}
{% raw %}example, in the Sermon of John, the Pharisee in the long robe to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right and the woman with the child are borrowed from the engravings of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the German master.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}where Lorenzo il Magnifico preserved a number of treasures of art{% endraw %}
