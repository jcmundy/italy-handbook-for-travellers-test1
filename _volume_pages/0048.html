{% raw %}ITALIAN ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The visitor to Venice will have an opportunity of tracing within{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a very limited space the progress of Renaissance architecture. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church of S. Zaccaria is an example of early Renaissance still in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conflict with Gothic, while the richly coloured church of S. Maria{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dei Miracoli and the Scuola di S. Marco exhibit the style in its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perfection. Foremost among the architects of Venice must be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mentioned the Lombardi, to whom most of the Venetian buildings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the 15th cent, are attributed ; but we shall afterwards advert to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the farther progress of Venetian architecture (p. xlv). One of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most famous architects of N. Italy was Fra Giocondo of Verona, a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monk, philologist (the discoverer of the letters of the younger Pliny),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a botanist, an engineer, and a thoroughly well trained architect, who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at a very advanced age, after the death of Bramante, was summoned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Rome to superintend the building of St. Peter's.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Examples of early Renaissance architecture abound in the towns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Tuscany. At Florence, the scene of Filippo Brunelleschi's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}labours (1379-1446), the attention is chiefly arrested by the church{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of S. Lorenzo (1425), with its two sacristies (the earlier by Brunel-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leschi, the later by Michael Angelo, which it is interesting to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}compare), while the small Cappella dei Pazzi near S. Croce is also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}noticeable. The Palazzo Rucellai is also important as showing the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}combination of pilasters with 'rustica', the greatest advance achieved{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the early Renaissance. Siena, with its numerous palaces, Pienza,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the model of a Renaissance town, and Urbino also afford excellent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}examples of the art of the Quattrocentists, but are beyond the limits{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the present volume. While all these different edifices possess{% endraw %}
{% raw %}many features in common, they may be classed in a number of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}groups, differing in material and various other characteristics, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entirely relieving them from any reproach of monotony.{% endraw %}
