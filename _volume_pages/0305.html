{% raw %}Brescia occupies a place of no little importance in the history of art{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from having given birth to Alessandro Bdonvicino, surnamed II Moretto{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1498-1555), who appears to have studied exclusively at his native place,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and whose teacher is said to have been Floriano Ferramola of Brescia. It{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has been asserted that he was influenced by Titian and the Roman school,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but for this there is no reason. Like the Veronese masters, he is distin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}guished from the Venetian school, with which he has generally been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}classed, by the comparative soberness of his colouring ('subdued silvery{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tone1), notwithstanding which he vies with the Venetians in richness and.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brilliancy, while he sometimes reveals the possession in full degree of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ideality of the golden period of art. Buonvicino began his career as a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painter in his 18th year. He rarely extended the sphere of his labours{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beyond his native place, and Brescia is therefore abundantly stored with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his works. The churches here (such as S. Clemente, p. 193) display his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fertility, both as a painter 'al fresco1 and in oils, forming quite a museum{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of his pictures. S. Giovanni Evangelista (p. 192), S. Nazaro e Celso{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 194), and the Galleria Tosio (p. 193) all contain admirable specimens{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of his powers. Another eminent master of the school of Brescia, and a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contemporary of Buonvicino, was Girol. Romanino (1485-1566). — Brescia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also contains several interesting antiquities (p. 192).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the station the town is entered at its S.W. corner by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Porta della Stazione (PI. A, 6), whence the Corso Vittorio Emmanuele{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leads N.E. to the Piazza Vecchia and the Piazza del Duomo.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}in 1604 by Lattanzio Gambara (but the dome not finally completed{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Passing through a door between the 2nd and 3rd altar, we{% endraw %}
{% raw %}descend by 25 steps to the Duomo Vecchio (PI. 6; D, 4), generally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}called La Rotonda, situated on the low ground to the S. of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Duomo Nuovo (if shut, apply to the sacristan of the new cathedral,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who lives at the back of the choir of the latter).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This massive structure is circular, as its name imports, with a pas¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sage round it, surmounted by a dome, and resting on eight short pillars{% endraw %}

{% raw %}dome and cupola (PiOmanesque) date from the 12th century. The tran¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sept and choir with lateral chapels at the back were added at a very{% endraw %}
{% raw %}early period. On both sides of the pulpit are statues by Alessandro Vittoria.{% endraw %}
