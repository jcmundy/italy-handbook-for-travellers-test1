{% raw %}384 M. Valence (Hotel du Louvre et de la Poste; Hotel de{% endraw %}
{% raw %}France; Rail. Restaurant), the Valentia of the ancients, once the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}capital of the Duchy of Valentinois, with which the infamous Caesar{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Borgia was invested by Louis XII. , is now the chief town of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Department of the Drome , with 23,300 inhabitants. It is pictur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}esquely situated, but contains nothing to interest the traveller. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the right bank lies St. Peray, famous for its wine.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}411 M. Montelimar (Rail. Restaurant). The ancient castle of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}once celebrated Monteil d'Adhemar family rises on an eminence from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the midst of mulberry-trees. The line here quits the Rhone ; the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plain on the right expands.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}443 M. Orange (*H6tel de la Poste), 3 M. from the Rhone,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Arausio of the Romans and once a prosperous and important{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place. In the middle ages it was the capital of a small prin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cipality, which, on the death of the last reigning prince without{% endraw %}
{% raw %}issue in 1531, fell to his nephew the Count of Nassau , and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}until the death of William III. (d. 1702), King of England, con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tinued subject to the house of Nassau-Orange. By the Peace of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Utrecht, Orange was annexed to France, and the house of Nassau{% endraw %}
{% raw %}retained the title only of princes of Orange. The antiquarian should{% endraw %}
{% raw %}if possible devote a few hours to the interesting Roman remains at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Orange. On the road to Lyons, ^4 M. to the N. of the town, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a well-preserved ^Triumphal Arch, with three archways and twelve{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns, probably dating from the close of the 2nd century. It is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adorned with reliefs of battles and of trophies consisting of weapons{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the prows of ships. On the S. side of the town , at the foot ot{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an eminence, lies the *Roman Theatre, 118 ft. in height, 338 ft. in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}length, with walls 13 ft. in thickness (concierge ^-l fr.). The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}admirably preserved wall of the stage still contains the three doors{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by which the actors entered; most of the tiers of seats for the spec¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tators, however, have entirely disappeared. The acoustic arrange¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment of the structure is admirable. Scanty remnants of a Circus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adjoin the theatre. The height above the theatre, once occupied{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the citadel of Orange which was destroyed by Louis XIV., affords{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a good survey of the neighbourhood. On the promenade is a statue{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the statesman Comte de Gasparin (d. 1862), a native of Orange.{% endraw %}
