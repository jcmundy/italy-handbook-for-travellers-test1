{% raw %}The right wall is devoted to the life of John the Baptist. The first scene,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Zacharias in the Temple, is celebrated for the number of portraits which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are introduced in a remarkably easy and life-like manner. The figures{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the right in the foreground are said to be portraits of Francesco Sas-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}setti, Andrea Medici, and Gianfrancesco Ridolfi, three famous merchants,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while to the left are Cristoforo Landini , Angelo Poliziano, Marsilio Fi-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cino, and Gentile de1 Becchi, distinguished scholars and humanists; the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}five men at the back, and to the right of Zacharias, are members of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tornabuoni family; the four figures by the angel are also said to be fa¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mily portraits. The other scenes are the Visitation, Nativity of John,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Naming of the child, the Baptist preaching repentance (in which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}master shows his art in grouping and individualising the figures), Birth{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Christ, and Dancing of the daughter of Herod. Several of these paint¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ings are unfortunately nearly obliterated. — The choir-stalls are by Baccio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}d'Agnolo, altered by Vasari. At the back of the altar is a *Slab to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}memory of Lionardo Dati by Ghiberti.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Chapel to the left of the choir, by Giuliano da Sangallo, contains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the celebrated wooden crucifix of Brunelleschi, which gave rise to the rivalry{% endraw %}
{% raw %}between him and his friend Donatello (p. 408). — The following Gaddi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Chapel , by Antonio Bosio, is adorned with the Raising of the daughter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Jairus, by A. Bronzino, and basreliefs by Bandini. — The Strozzi Cha¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pel in the left transept, to which steps ascend, contains ''Frescoes with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous figures; opposite the entrance the '-'Last Judgment, (1.) -'Paradise,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}over the figures in which broods a truly celestial repose, by Andrea Or-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cagna; Hell (r.), by his brother Bernardo ; altar-piece, Christ with saints,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}completed in 1357, by Andrea. — The next door, in the corner, leads to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the sacristy, the most interesting object in which is a *Fountain by Giov.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}della Robbia (1497), a magnificent work of its kind. — The altar-pieces{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the N. Aisle are of the 17th and 18th centuries. — In the Nave a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pulpit by Buggiano; stained glass by Alessandro Fiorentino (Botticelli?),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from drawings by Filippino Lippi.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the W. side of the church is the Sepolcreto, or burial vault, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an open colonnade and frescoes of the 14th cent , through which we enter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ancient Cloisters, called II Chiostro Vecchio, or Verde, adorned with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frescoes by Orcagna and Paolo Uccelli, in terra verde (different shades of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}green). Those by the latter were painted about 1446, those by Orcagna{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at an earlier date. The best-preserved is the Deluge, in which the artist{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has depicted with great power the helplessness of man in presence of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fury of the elements. — To the right in the cloisters is the Cappella{% endraw %}
{% raw %}degli Spagnuoli (best light, 10-12), formerly the chapter-house, begun in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1320, with frescoes of Giotto's school (attributed by Vasari to Taddeo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gaddi and Simone di Martino. but, according to Mr. Crowe, most of them{% endraw %}
{% raw %}probably by Andrea da Firenze, a few by Antonio Veneziano); on the wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the altar the Passion, and below it, Christ in Hades; on the ceiling{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Resurrection, Ascension, Descent of the Holy Ghost, Christ and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}doubting Peter on the water. On the E. side (r.) the Church militant{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and triumphant, the pope and emperor on the throne, surrounded by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their counsellors and illustrious men, such as Petrarch with Laura, Boc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}caccio, Cimabue, etc.; the heretics are represented as wolves hunted by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Dominicans in the form of black and white dogs ('Domini canes').{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the W. side (1.) Thomas Aquinas in his professorial capacity, sur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rounded by angels, prophets, and saints, in his hand an open book; at,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his feet the discomfited heretics Arius, Sabellius, and Averrhoes. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}niches 28 figures representing virtues and sciences. On the wall of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}door, History of St. Dominic and Martyrdom of St. Peter. — The Great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cloisters, the largest at Florence, with frescoes by Cigoli, Allori, Santi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}di Tito, Pocetti, and others, are adjacent to the above. — The three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}.smaller courts date from different parts of the 15th century.{% endraw %}
