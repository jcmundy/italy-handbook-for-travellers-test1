{% raw %}The First Door, the oldest of the three, on the S. side, opposite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Bigallo, was completed by Andrea Pisano in 1330 after 22 years of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}labour. The reliefs comprise scenes from the life of John the Baptist{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and allegorical representations of the eight cardinal virtues, square panels{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with tastefully executed borders. The figures, comparatively few in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}number, are full of vigorous life and charm by their naivete and grace.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The bronze decorations at the sides are by Vittore, the son of Lorenzo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ghiberti (1452-62); above is the Beheading of John the Baptist by Vincenzo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Danti, 1571.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Second Door (N.) was executed in 1403-24 by Lorenzo Ghiberti,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after a competition in which his designs were preferred to those of Jacopo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}della Quercia, Niccolo d'Arezzo, and Brunelleschi (comp. p. 403). Dona¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tello and Michelozzo were probably among his assistants in making the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}castings. The reliefs represent in 28 sections the history of Christ, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Apostles , and Fathers down to St. Augustine. They are quite equal to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}those of Andrea Pisano in clearness of arrangement, nobility of outline,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and tenderness of conception, while they surpass them in richness of pic¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}turesque life and in the harmony and variety of movement and expression{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the individual figures. The technical execution is simply perfect. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figure of St. John the Evangelist is the grandest in the series. Above{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the door, the *Preaching of St. John by Fr. Rustici, 1511 (supposed to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been designed by Leonardo da Vinci).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Third Door, facing the cathedral, also executed by Lorenzo Ghi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}berti (1425-52), is considered a marvel of art. It represents ten different{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scenes from scripture history: (1.) 1. Creation and Expulsion from Para¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dise; (r.) 2. Cain slaying his brother and Adam tilling the earth; 3.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Noah after the Flood, and his intoxication; 4. Abraham and the angels,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Sacrifice of Isaac; 5. Esau and Jacob; 6. Joseph and his brethren; 7.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Promulgation of the Law on Mt. Sinai; 8. The Walls of Jericho; 9. Battle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}against the Ammonites; 10. The Queen of Sheba. The artist has here{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wittingly transgressed the limits of the plastic art and produced what{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may be called a picture in bronze, but he has notwithstanding shed such{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a flood of loveliness over his creation that Michael Angelo pronounced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this door worthy of forming the entrance to Paradise. The beautiful{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bronze borders are also by Ghiberti, who has introduced his own portrait{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the central band (the man with the bald head, to the left, fourth from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the top). Over the door the *Baptism of Christ, by Andrea Sansovino, 1529;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the angels by Spinazzi (18th cent.). The two porphyry columns were pre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sented by the Pisans (in 1200) in recognition of the assistance rendered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them by the Florentines against Lucca in the expedition to Majorca in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1117. The chain of the harbour of Pisa, carried off by the Florentines{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1362, was formerly suspended here, but has been recently restored to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Pisans, and is preserved in their Campo Santo (p. 347).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Interior of the baptistery, below, are eight niches, each con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}taining two columns of Oriental granite with gilded capitals. Above is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gallery with Corinthian pilasters and double windows. The whole arran¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gement shows that its builder was well acquainted with ancient form?,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and seems to point to an earlier building, of which part of the triumphal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arch in the choir is a relic. (Local tradition affirms that a temple of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mars originally occupied this site.) The dome, which is 90 ft. in diameter,{% endraw %}
