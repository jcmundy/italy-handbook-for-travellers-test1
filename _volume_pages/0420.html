{% raw %}Parma owes its importance in the History of Art to Antonio Allegri{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Correggio (1494-1534; p. 289), who lived here in a quiet and modest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}style, and died early. It was not till a later period, when he was followed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Carracci, that his merits were duly appreciated (characteristics, see{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. lviii). The best-known of his pupils is Francesco Mazzuola, surnamed Par-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}meggianino (1503-40), an excellent portrait-painter, and a native of Parma.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}*Cathedral {II Duomo; PL 1 ; F, 3), an admirable example of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Lombard-Romanesque style, begun in 1060, but not completed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}till the 13th century. It is a cruciform building covered with a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dome, with a somewhat raised choir above a crypt, and abroad facade{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a triple columnar gallery. The three portals are embellished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with two huge lions (executed in 1281 by Bono da Bisone) and four{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of smaller size, and sculptures by Lucchino Bianchini, 1493.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Interior, consisting of nave and aisles, rests on fourteen articulat¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed pillars, above which runs a fine triforium. The vaulting of the nave{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was painted by Girolamo Mazzuola. 3rd chapel on the right, a Descent from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Cross in relief by Benedetto Antelami (1178); 4th chapel, frescoes of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 15th cent.; 5th chapel, frescoes by Rondani, a pupil of Correggio.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the right of the steps to the choir is the Cappella S. Agata with an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}altar-piece by Gatti, and on the right a bust of Petrarch, who was archdean{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the cathedral, a work of 1713.{% endraw %}
