{% raw %}Art and Science. The proud position occupied by Florence in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}history of art and science was first established by Dante Alighieri, born{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here in 1265, author of the 'Divine Comedy', and the great, founder of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the modern Italian language. In 1302 he was banished with his party,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and in 1321 died at Ravenna. Giovanni Boccaccio, the first expounder of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the illustrious Dante, and celebrated for his 'Decamerone', which served{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as a model for the 'Canterbury Tales' of Chaucer, also lived at Florence.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Florence, too, was the chief cradle of the school of the Humanists (15th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cent.), who aimed at a universal and harmonious development of the per¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sonal character, and whose contemplative life was far exalted above{% endraw %}
{% raw %}every-day realities. This was the home of Salutato, Leonardo Brum', and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Marsuppini, the infidel, whose firmly moulded characters recall the per¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sonages of antiquity; it was here that the sources of classic literature were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}re-discovered by Niccolb de'' Niccoli, Traversari, and other enthusiastic col¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lectors of books; it was here that the Platonic Academy developed the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}study of the antique into a species of religious worship, and most of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}humanists, including Ficino, Poggio, Landini, and Pico della Mirandola,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who resided here for longer or shorter periods, enjoyed promotion and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the highest distinction at Florence. Even after the decline of 'humanism1{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Florence continued to surpass the rest of Italy in intellectual culture, as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the names of Macchiavelli, Varchi, Guicciardini, and Galileo testify.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the development of the Fine Arts Florence has played so im¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portant a part, that her art-history is in many respects nearly coincident{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with that of the whole of Italy. We therefore refer the reader to our{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prefatory article on the subject, and shall now merely direct his atten¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion to those points which more specially concern Florence. In the 13th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cent. , when frequent changes of the constitution, and constantly recur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ring dissensions of factions, began to take place, and when private citi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}zens for the first time manifested an interest in public life, a general{% endraw %}
{% raw %}taste for art gradually sprang up at Florence. With characteristic pride{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Florentines proceeded to erect their cathedral, which was begun by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Arnolfo del Cambio (1240-1311), and in the form of their Palazzo Vecchio,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the restless aspect of their political life is distinctly reflected. The la¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bours of Cimabue (1240?-1302?), and particularly those of Giotto (1276{% endraw %}
{% raw %}-1337) at length entitled Florence to be regarded as the headquarters of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Italian painting of the 14th cent., while the journeys undertaken by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Giotto from Padua to Naples were the means of rendering his style{% endraw %}
{% raw %}predominant throughout the peninsula. Among Giotto's most distin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}guished pupils we may mention Taddeo and Agnolo Gaddi, Andrea di{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Clone (Orgagna, or Orcagna), who was also noted as an architect, Spi-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nello Aretino, Giottino, and Stefano. After this school had flourished for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nearly a century, the Renaissance began to dawn in the 3rd decade of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 15th century. While Brunelleschi (1379-1416) had adhered to the nation¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}al traditions in his palatial architecture (Palazzo Pitti), he derived{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous suggestions for his churches from a study of the antique, par¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ticularly in the execution of details. His successors were Leo Battista{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alberti (1405-72), Michelozzo (1391-1472), Benedetto da Majano, and Cro-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}naca. Stimulated by the example of the humanists, the artists of this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period aimed at versatility, and were not content to confine their labours{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to one sphere of art; so that we frequently hear of architects who were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the same time sculptors, and sculptors and goldsmiths who were also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painters. Among the most distinguished Florentine sculptors of the Re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}naissance were Luca della Robbia (1400-82), who has given his name{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the glazed reliefs in terracotta, Lorenzo Ghiberti (1378-1455), and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above all Donatello (1386-1466), who exercised a great influence on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}development of Italian sculpture, and is justly regarded as the precursor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Michael Angelo. The energetic life and strong individuality of his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figures are such, that their deficiency in gracefulness is well nigh for¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gotten. Beside these celebrated sculptors there were many of inferior{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reputation, who were fully occupied both here and at Rome in the exe¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cution of tombstones.{% endraw %}
