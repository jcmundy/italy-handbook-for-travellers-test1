{% raw %}The next sculptor of note was Andrea Verrocchio (1435-88).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Most of the other masters of this period (Antonio Rossellino,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mino da Fiesole , Desiderio da Settignano) were chiefly oc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cupied in the execution of tombstones, and do not occupy a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}position of much importance; but the life and sense of beauty which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}characterise the early Renaissance are admirably exemplified in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}works of the comparatively unknown Matteo Civitali of Lucca{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1435-1501; Altar of St. Regulus in the Cathedral, p. 353).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Important Florentine masters of the first half of the 16th cent.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were Giov. Franc. Rustici(1474-1550?), who was perhaps inspir¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed by Leonardo, and particularly Andrea Sansovino (1460-1529),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the author of the exquisite group of Christ and the Baptist in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Baptistery at Florence, of superb monuments at Rome (in the choir{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of S. Maria del Popolo), and of part of the sculptures which adorn{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Santa Casa at Loreto. Northern Italy also contributed largely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the development of the plastic art. The Certosa at Pavia , for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}example, afforded occupation during several decades to numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}artists, among whom the most eminent were Giovanni Antonio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amadeo (sculptor of the huge monuments in the Cappella Colleoni{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Bergamo), and, at a later period, Cristoforo Solari, surnamed{% endraw %}

{% raw %}afforded employment to Mazzoni and Begarelli (p. 296), artists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in terracotta, the latter of whom is sometimes compared with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Correggio.{% endraw %}
