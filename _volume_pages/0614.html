{% raw %}Then across several rocky ridges in 2 hrs. more to the Trigione, the last{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spur of the Rotondo, a wilderness of blocks of granite. The crater-shaped,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}snowcapped summit is visible hence; below it lies the small and clear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lago di Monte Rotondo; in the foreground, green pastures. Fields of snow{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and ice, rising from the lake, must be laboriously traversed (2 hrs.) be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fore the summit is attained. A magnificent ''Panorama is here enjoyed.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The spectator surveys the greater part of the island; N. the Capo Corso;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}W. the bays of Porto, Sagona, and Ajaccio; E. the blue Mediterranean,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the islands of Monte Cristo, Pianosa, Capraia, and Elba, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mainland of Italy ; then the white Alpes Maritimes, extending from Toulon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Nice to Genoa. Corsica itself resembles a vast rocky relief-map; its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}principal mountain-chains, with their rivers and valleys, are distinctly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recognisable. Towards the S., however, the view is obstructed by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}massive Monto d'Oro. The descent may be made on the side next to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lago di Pazzolo, where the dark rocky pyramid of the Frate (monk) rises.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Violet and forget-me-not, (here popularly called the 'marvellous flower of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the mountains') grow abundantly in the rocky clefts on the banks of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lake. The mufflone, the wild horned sheep of Corsica, of a dark brown{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colour, with silky hair, browses on these lofty summits. The huts of Co{% endraw %}
{% raw %}di Mozzo may now be regained in 3 hrs., and Corte in 4-5 hrs. more.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The road reaches the Golo, the chief river of the island, at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(66 M.) Ponte alia Leccia.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A road leads hence to Calvi, 4672 M. distant, to which a diligence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}runs. It, proceeds to the E. by Morosaglia and Porta, and descends{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the coast. Several miles higher up lies the district of Rostino, or Mo¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rosaglia, the native place of the Paoli family. A dilapidated cottage is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still pointed out, in the hamlet of Stretta, as that in which Pasquale{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Paoli was born in 1724. His father Hyacinth was a physician and poet,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and at the same time the leader of the Corsicans. Anecdotes of his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}noble and heroic character are still current in this district; his memory{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is also perpetuated by a school, established in an old monastery at Moro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}saglia with funds bequeathed by him for the purpose. A room in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monastery was once occupied by Pasquale Paoli as his study, and here his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}elder brother Clement, once a general, afterwards a monk, died in 1793.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The road to Bastia follows the right bank of the Golo, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it crosses at (72 M.) Pontenuovo. The Golo is followed as far as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the point where the road unites with that from Bonifacio (p. 457).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A road leads S. from the river to (5 M.) the village of Vescovato,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situated among mountains and forests of chestnuts, and containing the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}house of Pietro Filippini, the Corsican historian of the 16th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Here also is situated the chateau of Count Matteo Buttafuoco (now the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}property of the family of Marshal Sebastiani), who when a young French{% endraw %}
{% raw %}officer invited Rousseau to Vescovato, in consequence of the following{% endraw %}
{% raw %}passage in his 'Contrat Social' alluding to the Corsicans: 'The vigour and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perseverance with which these brave people have succeeded in gaining and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}defending their liberty merit, for them that some wise man should teach{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them how to preserve it. I have a certain presentiment that this island{% endraw %}
{% raw %}will one day cause astonishment to the whole of Europe'. The latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prediction was fulfilled 20 years after Rousseau's death (1778), although{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in a very different manner from that which the philosopher had in view.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The road now leads direct to the N. ; the coast-district is flat{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and full of lagoons.{% endraw %}
