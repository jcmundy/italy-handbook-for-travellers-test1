{% raw %}The representation of the beginning of Christ's sorrows by the Corruption{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Judas (to the left of the choir-wall) is a fine dramatic touch. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Crucifixion Giotto has not only surpassed all his predecessors in the no¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bility of his conception of the person of the Divine Sufferer, but has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}added, a most affecting and pathetic feature in the numerous cherubs,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who show every degree of sympathy and sorrow. The gem of the whole{% endraw %}
{% raw %}series, however, is the Pieta, or the dead body of Christ wept over by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Virgin and her friends. The tone of the composition is in admirable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}keeping with its tragic content. — The Lowest Row consists of allegori¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cal figures of the Virtues and Vices in grisaille, and leads up naturally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Last Judgment, the Vices standing on the same side as Hell, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Virtues on that of Paradise. The painting of Christ enthroned with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}angels, above the choir-arch, shows that Giotto was as much at home in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portraying forms of placid gracefulness as in the domains of passion and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}emotion. — The Frescoes in the Choir (Glorification of the Virgin) are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by a later hand, and of little importance. (Photographs from the ori i-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nals may be purchased of Naya at Venice, 3 fr. each, see p. 231.) — At{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the back of the altar is the monument of the founder of the church, Gio¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vanni Pisano, 1321.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Near the Porta Codalunga, in the vicinity, is the church of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}I Carmini (PL 6; G, 3), with a dome and large choir with six{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chapels on each side, and an unfinished facade. — In the adjacent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}open space rises a monument to Petrarch, erected by the town on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}18th June, 1874, the 500th anniversary of his death.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the right is the *Scuola del Carmine (PL 24; G, 3; now a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}baptistery; sacristan in the cloisters), with sadly-damaged frescoes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the lives of Christ and SS. Joachim, Anna, and Mary.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}the frescoes in the Scuola del Santo (p. 221), 'a hasty work unworthy of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the master'; Girolamo da Santa Croce, Birth of Mary, Presentation in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Temple, Purification, and Sposalizio; the others by Paduan masters. "Al¬{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Vecchio.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Palazzo Giustiniani, ViaPeusio, contains a private picture-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gallery, including several portraits by Titian (among others the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sketches for the portraits of Philip II. and Francis I.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Cathedral (PL 11; E, 2), with a plain facade, was built by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Righetlo and Della Valle about 1550. The Baptistery (PL 3; E, 2),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adjoining it on the N., a brick structure of the 12th cent., is adorned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with frescoes of 1380; the sacristy contains some miniatures of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}12-15th centuries. — Adjacent is the Library of the Cathedral{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Chapter, containing an interesting painting by Semitecolo of Venice{% endraw %}
