{% raw %}90 Route 15.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}GENOA.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Harbour.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the Cava, below Carignano (PI. F, 5, 6), a large new Molo is to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be constructed, while the present Molo Nuovo is to be so lengthened{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that it will continue to be the outer pier. Extensive quays con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nected by rails with the main line are also projected, with a view{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to enable the largest vessels to unload without lighters.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the E. side of the present harbour, near the Piazza Cavour{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the Via Vittorio Emmanuele (PL E, 4), lies the enclosed Porto{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Franco with its extensive bonded warehouses (visitors admitted).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Dogana (PL 3; E, 3) occupies the building of the former Banco{% endraw %}
{% raw %}di S. Giorgio (p. 88). The large hall is embellished with statues of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}men who have deserved well of the town, some of them of the 15th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}century. On the upper floor are the Archives.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The central part of the harbour is bordered by a lofty wall with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arcades, the marble platform of which, called the *Terrazzo di{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Marmo, 20 paces in width, affords an excellent promenade, especially{% endraw %}
{% raw %}early in the morning. There are two approaches to the terrace,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one opposite the Hotel de la Ville (PL c; E, 3; known as the 'Scala{% endraw %}
{% raw %}della Rotonda'), and another to the N., opposite the Hotel des Quatre{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nations (PL d; E, 2, 3), both of which are closed at dusk. — A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}row in the harbour, for which numerous boatmen offer their ser¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vices, is also recommended (2 fr. per hour for 1-4 persons, but a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bargain should be made).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Near the end of the Via Vittorio Emanuele, on the E. side, is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church of S. Giorgio, a rococo structure with a dome. Adjoining it is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}another smaller and tasteful church, with rococo details; the interior is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also fine. A little farther on is the small Piazza Cattaneo , with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palace of that name, a room in which contains eight pictures by Van Dyck.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Via delle Grazie leads hence to the Gothic church of S. Cosmo, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contains a Florentine Madonna of the 14th cent, (to the left of the high-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}altar). — Continuing to ascend beyond S. Cosmo, we reach the church of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sta. Maria di Castello (PL 16; E, 4), occupying the site of an ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman castle. Above the portal is an ancient architrave, and ten of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shafts of the columns in the interior are also of very early date. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first chapel to the left contains a Roman sarcophagus, now used as an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}altar, and the last chapel contains a marble door with Renaissance sculp¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tures. The choir was added in the 15th century. In the transept is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Madonna by Justus d'Allamagna, 1451 (under glass).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Instead of walking through the noisy and bustling streets near{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Terrazzo di Marmo, the traveller is recommended to take the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}following route. Leaving the piazza of the station, we descend by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a lane opposite the corner of the Hotel de Londres to the Via di Pre{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL D, E, 2), which we follow. At the corner to the right stands{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the small Romanesque church of S. Giovanni Battista, which in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}consequence of a change of front now possesses two apses (morning{% endraw %}
{% raw %}light most favourable). Adjacent is the Piazza della Commenda,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the Oratory of the same name, a decayed Renaissance struc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ture. We then cross the Piazza della Darsena, from which the Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}della Fontana leads, to the left, to the Annunziata (p. 96), and pass{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into the Via del Campo (PL E, 2, 3) through the fine Gothic Porta{% endraw %}
{% raw %}de' Vacca, which is embellished with mediaeval scupturcs and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towers of the 16th century. From the Piazza Fossatello (PL E, 3){% endraw %}
