{% raw %}on the site of the former church of S. Geminiano. The ground-floors{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of these structures consist of arcades, in which the cafes and shops{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mentioned at pp. 229, 231 are established. — The Piazza of St.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mark is the grand focus of attraction at Venice. On summer-even¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ings, after sunset, all who desire to enjoy fresh air congregate here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The scene is most animated towards 8 p.m., especially on the even¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ings when the military band plays (Sundays, Mondays, Wednes¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}days, and Fridays, 8-10 o'clock), when the Piazza is sometimes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thronged until after midnight. On other evenings the crowd dis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perses about 10 o'clock. In winter the band plays on the same days,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2-4 p.m., and the Piazza is then a fashionable promenade. Early in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the morning a few visitors to the cafes may be seen sipping their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coffee, but these are rarely natives of Venice. The Venetians{% endraw %}
{% raw %}themselves are seldom visible at a very early hour, and the Piazza{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is comparatively deserted except at the hours just mentioned. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Piazza with its adjuncts presents a strikingly imposing appearance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by moonlight.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A large flock of Pigeons resorts daily to the Piazza at 2 p.m. to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fed. According to tradition, Admiral Dandolo, while besieging Candia at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the beginnine of the 13th cent., received intelligence from the island by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}means of carrier-pigeons, which greatly facilitated its conquest. He then{% endraw %}
{% raw %}despatched the birds to Venice with the news of his success, and since{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that period their descendants have been carefully tended and highly re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vered by the citizens. They nestle in the nooks and crannies of the sur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rounding buildings, and are generally seen in great numbers in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}evening, perched on the facade of St. Mark's.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The three lofty Flagstaffs (Pili) in front of the church, rising from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pedestals resembling candelabra, executed by Aless. Leopardo in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1505, once bore the banners of the kingdoms of Cyprus, Candia,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the Morea, to commemorate their subjugation by the republic.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On Sundays and festivals the Italian colours are now hoisted here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The nucleus of **S. Marco (PL 17; E, 4), the Church of St.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mark, the tutelary saint of Venice, whose remains are said to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been brought by Venetian citizens from Alexandria in 828, consists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a Romanesque building of the 10th and 11th cent., the brick{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls of which are now concealed by a lining of marble. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}12th and following cent, it was remodelled in a Byzantine style,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and decorated with the lavish and almost Oriental magnificence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which now excites our admiration and wonder. The facade received{% endraw %}
{% raw %}some additions in the Gothic style in the 15th cent., which en¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hanced the fantastic effect of the whole. The form of the edifice is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that of a Greek cross (with equal arms), covered by a Byzantine{% endraw %}
