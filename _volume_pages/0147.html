{% raw %}wide squares, and numerous gardens. Its history explains this. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plan of the old town, with slight variations, is ascertained to be the same{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as that of the colony founded by the Emperor Augustus. It formed a rec¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tangle of 1370 ft. in length, and 2210 ft. in breadth, and is now inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sected by the Via di Dora Grossa, which runs between the Piazza Castello{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the Via della Consolata. It had four principal gates, of which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Porta Palalina, to the N. (in the Palazzo delle Torri, PL 44) still exists.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The whole town was comprised within this circumference during the middle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ages, until in the 17th cent., under the princes of Savoy, a systematic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extension of the city was begun in accordance with the original plan.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The fortifications constructed by Francis I. in 1536, and finally the siege{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of 1706 cleared away most of the old buildings, and gave the town its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}present appearance. The fortifications were demolished by the French{% endraw %}
{% raw %}when in possession of the city and environs in 1801, and the citadel had{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to give place to the railway in 1857.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The spacious Piazza Castello (PI. E, F, 2), with the Royal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Palace, forms the centre of the town. From this point the busiest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}streets diverge: — the Via Roma, the Via di Dora Grossa (or Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Garibaldi), and the broad and handsome Via di Po, leading to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bridge over the Po, and flanked by arcades (Portici), containing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shops, the handsomest of which are near the Piazza Castello (those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the direction of the Po, towards the Piazza Vittorio Emmanuele,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being inferior). These arcades present a busy and brilliant scene{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the evening, when lighted by gas. The University in the Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}di Po, see p. 70. — In the S.E. angle of the Piazza Castello is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}new Galleria dell' Industria Subalpina, containing cafes, a large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}birraria, and concert rooms, which deserves a visit, though inferior{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the arcade at Milan. The other end of the arcade is in the Piazza{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Carlo Alberto (p. 65).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Palazzo Madama (PL 39; E, 2), the ancient castle, a lofty{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and cumbrous pile in the centre of the Piazza Castello , is the only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mediaeval structure of which Turin boasts, and was erected by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}William of Monferrat, when master of the town in the latter half of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 13th century. It owes its present name to the mother of King{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Victor Amadeus II., who as Dowager Duchess (lMadama ReaW){% endraw %}
{% raw %}occupied the building, and embellished it in 1718 by the addition{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a handsome double flight of steps and the facade with marble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns on the W. side, from a design by Juvara. The two original{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tqjvers on the E. side are still standing; two others on the W. side,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one of which contains an observatory, are concealed by the facade.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Down to 1865 the Palazzo Madama was the seat of the Italian sen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ate, and it now contains several institutions. — In front of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Palace stands a Monument to the Sardinian Army (PL 24) by Vine.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vela, erected by the Milanese in 1859.{% endraw %}
