{% raw %}Climate. Ajaccio is admirably sheltered by lofty hills on the N.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}N.E., and E., but is sometimes visited by violent winds from the S.W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The mean annual temperature is about 3° Fahr. higher than that of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Riviera. The heat is somewhat tempered by the humidity of the atmo¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sphere. The number of rainy days is comparatively small (averaging{% endraw %}
{% raw %}34 in the six winter months) but a heavy dew falls at night. For those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}afflicted with pulmonary complaints Ajaccio offers one great advantage in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its complete immunity from dust owing to the hard granitic soil. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accommodation for visitors is rapidly improving.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ajaccio (pronounced Ajassio in French), with 16,500 inhab.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was founded by the Genoese in 1492, and made the capital of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the island in 1811 by Napoleon, at the request of his mother{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Letitia. It is most beautifully situated in an extensive bay, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stretches N. to the Punta della Parata, near the Isole Sanguinarie,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and S. to the Capo di Muro, whilst the background is formed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}imposing mountains, often covered with a snowy mantle until late{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the summer. The town presents a somewhat deserted aspect,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}although great improvements have taken place of late years.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On quitting the harbour we first reach the broad Place du Mar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}che, adorned with a fountain surmounted by a marble statue of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Napoleon I. as First Consul. To the right, at the point where the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Boulevart Roi Jerome diverges, is situated the Hotel-de-Ville. On{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the first floor is the * Reception Hall, adorned with busts and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pictures illustrative of the history of the family of Napoleon.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Rue Fesch leads to the College Fesch, which contains a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}large collection of pictures (600, most of them copies), casts, a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}library, and a collection of Corsican minerals. The court contains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a bronze statue of Cardinal Fesch, half-brother of Napoleon's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mother, by whom the collection was bequeathed to the town. Ad-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}joing the College is the Chapelle Fesch, built in 1855, containing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the tombs of Letitia Ramolino, mother of Napoleon ('mater regum';{% endraw %}
{% raw %}d. at Rome in 1836), and of Cardinal Fesch (d. at Rome in 1839).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}By the street to the left opposite the fountain, and then by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the third transverse street to the right, we reach the small Place{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Letizia, containing the house in which Napoleon was born, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the inscription: '■Napoleon est ne dans cette maison le 15 AoUt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1769' (the concierge lives opposite, fee lfr.). It contains a few{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reminiscences of the great warrior.{% endraw %}
