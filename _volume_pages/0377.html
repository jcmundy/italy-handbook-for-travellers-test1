{% raw %}**24. Titian, Assumption (Assunta), painted in 1516-18 for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Frari (p. 266), whose high-altar it once adorned.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The present position of the picture is very unfavourable. 'Neither{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the place nor the light is that for which Titian intended it; and the con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trast between the radiance of the sky and the darkness round the tomb{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is lost on the one hand, whilst coarseness of outline and foreshortening{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— unseen in the gloom of a church — are forced unfairly into view.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Yet few pictures impress us more even now with the master's power. . . .{% endraw %}
{% raw %}There is nothing so remarkable in this enchanting picture as the contrast{% endraw %}
{% raw %}between the apparent simplicity of the results, and the science with which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these results are brought about. Focal concentration is attained by per¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spective science, applied alike to lines and to atmosphere, at the same{% endraw %}
{% raw %}time that a deep and studied intention is discoverable in the subtle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distribution of radiance and gloom. . . . Something indescribable strikes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}us in the joyful innocence of the heavenly company whose winged units{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crowd together singing, playing, wondering and praying, some in light,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}some in half light, others in gloom, with a spirit of life moving in them{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that is quite delightful to the mind and the eye. Like the bees about{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their queen this swarm of angels rises with the beauteous apparition of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Virgin, whose noble face is transfigured with gladness, whose step is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}momentarily arrested as she ascends on the clouds, and with upturned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}face and outstretched arms longs for the heaven out of which the Eternal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}looks down. To this central point in the picture Titians invites us by all{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the arts of which he is a master. . . . The apostles we observed are in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shade. An awfully inspired unanimity directs their thoughts and eyes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the tomb round which they linger to the circle of clouds beauti¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fully supported in its upward passage by the floating shapes of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}angels.' The lifelike semblance of nature in these forms and the mar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vellous power with which their various sensations of fear, devotion, re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}verent wonder, and rapture are expressed, raise Titian to a rank as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}high as that held by Raphael and Michaelangelo. — G. & G.{% endraw %}
