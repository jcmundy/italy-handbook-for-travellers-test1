{% raw %}VIII. Hotels.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}First Class Hotels, comfortably fitted up, are to be found at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}all the principal resorts of travellers in Northern Italy, most of them{% endraw %}
{% raw %}having fixed charges: room 2y2-5 fr., bougie 75 c. to 1 fr., atten¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dance (exclusive of the 'facchino' and porter) lfr., table d'hote{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4-6 fr. The charge for dinner does not include wine, which is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}usually poor and dear. For a prolonged stay an agreement may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}generally be made with the landlord for pension at a more moder¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ate rate. Visitors are expected to dine at the table d'hote; other¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wise the charge for rooms is apt to be raised. The cuisine is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mixture of French and Italian. The charge for the use of the hotel-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}omnibus from the station to the hotel is so high (l-l1/^ fr-)> that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it is often cheaper to take a cab. It is also easier for those who use{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a cab to proceed to another hotel, should they not like the rooms{% endraw %}
{% raw %}offered them. Rooms on the ground-floor should be avoided.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Second Class Hotels are thoroughly Italian in their ar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rangements, and are rarely very clean or comfortable. The charges{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are little more than one-half of the above. They have no table{% endraw %}
{% raw %}d'hote, but there is generally a trattoria connected with the house,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where refreshments a la carte, or a dinner a prezzo fisso, may be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}procured at any hour. Morning coffee, especially in the smaller{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towns, is usually taken at a cafe" and not at the inn. It is everywhere{% endraw %}
{% raw %}customary to make enquiries beforehand as to the charges for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rooms, not forgetting the servizio e candela. These inns will often{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be found convenient and economical by the voyageur en garcon,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the better houses of this class may be visited by ladies; but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the new-comer should frequent hotels of the first class only.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Pbnsions of Venice and Florence also receive passing tra¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vellers, but as the price of dejeuner is usually (though not uni¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}versally) included in the fixed daily charge, the traveller has either{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to sacrifice some of the best hours for visiting the galleries or to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pay for a meal he does not consume.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hotels Garnis are to be found in most of the larger towns,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with charges for rooms similar to those in the second-class hotels.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Private Apartments are recommended for a prolonged stay.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A distinct agreement as to rent should be made beforehand. When{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a whole suite of apartments is hired, a written contract on stamped{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paper should be drawn up with the aid of some one acquainted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the language and customs of the place (e.g. a banker), in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}order that 'misunderstandings' may be prevented. For single tra¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vellers a verbal agreement with regard to attendance, linen, stoves{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and carpets in winter, a receptacle for coal, and other details will{% endraw %}
{% raw %}generally suffice. Comp. p. xxvi.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Money and other valuables should either be carried on the per¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}son or entrusted to the landlord in exchange for a receipt.{% endraw %}
