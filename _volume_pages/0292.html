{% raw %}The manufacturers of the far-famed Violins and Violas of Cremona{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were Andr. and Ant. Amati (1590-1620), the two Guarneri (1552-80 and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1717-40), and Stradivari (1670-1728).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cremona was the birthplace of Sofonisbe Angussola (1535-1626), who,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}like her five sisters, practised the art of painting, and was highly esteemed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by her contemporaries. She afterwards retired to Genoa, and even in her{% endraw %}
{% raw %}old age attracted the admiration of Van Dyck. In the 16th cent. Cremons{% endraw %}
{% raw %}possessed a school of art, of its own, which appears to have been influenced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Romanino especially, and also by Giulio Romano.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Piazza Grande (PL F, 4) rises the Torrazzo, a tower{% endraw %}
{% raw %}397 ft. in height, said to be the loftiest in Italy, erected in 1261-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}84, connected with the cathedral by a series of logge. The sum¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mit commands an extensive prospect. — Opposite the tower is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Gothic *Palazzo Pubblico (PL 12) of 1245 (restored), containing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a few pictures by masters of the Cremona school, and a richly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}decorated chimney-piece in marble by G. C. Redone (1502). Ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}jacent is the *Palazzo de'' Giureconsulti, of 1292, now a school.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Cathedral (PL 3 ; F, 4), of 1107, in the German-Lombard{% endraw %}
{% raw %}style, has a rich facade embellished with columns.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Interior with its aisles and transept, also flanked with aisles,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is covered with frescoes executed by various representatives of the school{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Cremona, such as Boccaccino (1500), father and son, and the later masters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Campi, Altobello, Bembo, and Gatti. On the left wall: above the first four{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arches of the nave, Boccaccino the Elder, Life of the Virgin, depicted in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}eight scenes; 5th arch, Bonifazio Bembo, The Magi, and Presentation in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Temple; beyond the organ, Altobello di Melone, Flight into Egypt, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Massacre of the Innocents; above the last arch, Boccaccino, Christ teaching{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the Temple. The colossal figures in the apse are also by Boccaccino.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Right wall, above the arches: Altobello, Last Supper, Christ washing the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}feet of the Disciples, Christ on the Mount of Olives, Christ taken by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}soldiers, Christ before Caiaphas; above the 4th arch, Cristoforo Moretto{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cremonese, Christ led out to be crucified, Scourging of Christ; 5th arch,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Romanino, Crown of Thorns, Ecce Homo; above the last three arches,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towards the facade, *Pordenones three celebrated Passion Scenes , Christ{% endraw %}
{% raw %}before Pilate, Christ and Veronica, Christ nailed to the Cross. On the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}front wall are a colossal Crucifixion and Entombment by Pordenone. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The two pulpits are embellished with important Lombardic reliefs, taken{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from an old altar, and ascribed to Amadeo.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the vicinity are the octagonal Battistero (PL 1; F, 4) of 1167,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the Campo Santo (PL 2), with curious and very ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosaics ; among these are Hercules and Nessus; Piety wounded by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cruelty; Faith tearing out the tongue of Discord, etc. (entrance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the right of the cathedral, No. 7).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the Palazzo Pubblico to the W. the Contrada Ariberti{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leads to the Palazzo Reale (formerly A la di Ponzone), which contains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}natural history and other collections, a cabinet of coins, and a few{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pictures (open daily 9-3, except Sundays). Farther up the Corso{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vittorio Emmanuele, in the second cross-street to the left, is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}richly-painted church of S. Pietro al Pb (PL 10; D, E, 5), designed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Ripari in 1549-70, and containing pictures by Ant. Campi, Bern.{% endraw %}
