{% raw %}The **Canal Grande ('■Canalazzo'1), the main artery of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traffic of Venice, nearly 2 M. in length, and 33-66 yds. in width,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intersects the city from N.W. to S.E., dividing it into two unequal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}parts, and resembling an inverted S in shape. The Canal Grande{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occupies the same position at Venice as the Corso at Rome, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Toledo at Naples, or the Boulevards at Paris. Hundreds of gondolas{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and other craft are seen here gliding in every direction. Small{% endraw %}
{% raw %}steamboats ply every */2 hr. from the Citta, di Monaco (p. 229) to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the railway-station, calling on the way at the Academy, S. Toma,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Riva del Carbon, and S. Geremia (fares, whole way 20 c, shorter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distances 10 c). On Sundays, between 7 and 9 p.m., a barge{% endraw %}
{% raw %}containing a military band traverses the Canal Grande, followed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous gondolas. Handsome houses and magnificent palaces{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rise on the banks, for this is the street of the Nobili, the ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aristocracy of Venice. A trip on the canal is extremely interesting;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the distance from the Piazzetta to the station may be traversed in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}less than ^ hr. , but 3/4-l hr. at least should be devoted to it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in order to obtain a glimpse at the principal palaces in passing.{% endraw %}
