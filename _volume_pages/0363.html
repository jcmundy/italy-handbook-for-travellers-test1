{% raw %}One of his contemporaries was Vittore Carpaccio (d. after 1519), a lively{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The first of the great masters was Giorgione (Barbarella, 1477?-1511),{% endraw %}

{% raw %}at Florence). The peculiar glow of his colouring, an attribute which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seems rather to be natural to him than acquired from others, imparts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}even to his isolated half-figures an unwonted life and poetical charm.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The first artist who fully developed that type of female beauty in which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a simple and natural enjoyment of life is so admirably expressed, was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Jacopo Palma (Vecchio, 1480-1528). Surpassing all his fellows in reputation,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in fertility, and in the length of his career, next comes the great Tiziano{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vecellio (1477-1575). His finest frescoes are in the Scuola del Santo and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Scuola del Carmine at Padua, and though most of his oil-paintings are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distributed throughout the galleries of Europe, several of his most strik¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing works, especially in the province of religious composition, are still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preserved at Venice.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Such was the vitality and vigour, and so great the resources of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Venetian School at this period, that even masters of secondary importance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frequently produced works of almost unrivalled excellence. Those who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chiefly call for notice are Sebastiano del Piombo (1485 -1547), who was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}afterwards influenced by the fascinating proximity of Michael Angelo,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rocco Marconi, Lorenzo Lotto, Bonifacio, Giovanni Antonio (da) Pordenone{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(d. in 1539), whose carnation - tints are unsurpassed, and Paris Bordone{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1500-70), whose portraits rival those of Titian. A prominent master of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}somewhat younger generation was Jacopo Tintoretto (Robusti, 1518-94), who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}squandered his eminent abilities on superficial works, and in his eager¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ness for effect threw away the rich golden tints which formed a dis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tinctive characteristic of his school. Paolo Caliari, surnamed Veronese,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1528-86), on the other hand, though more material in his conceptions,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and frequently confining himself to mere representations of actual life,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was a faithful adherent to the traditions of his predecessors. Among the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}last masters of note were the Bassano's, Palma Giovane, and Padovanino,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after whose time the Venetian school became extinct. In the 18th cent.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}however, we must mention Tiepolo (d. 1770), a spirited decorative painter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Antonio Ganale, an architectural painter, and his pupil Bern. Belotto, both{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surnamed Canaletto, who were much admired by their contemporaries.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Venice, situated iu 45° 25' N. latitude, lies 2*/2 M. from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mainland in the Lagune, a shallow part of the Adriatic about 25 M.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in length and 9 M. in width. The 15,000 houses and palaces of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Venice are situated on three large and 114 small islands, formed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by 150 canals, connected by 378 bridges (most of them of stone),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and altogether about 7 M. in circumference. The population which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had dwindled from 200,000 to 96,000 after its dissolution as an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}independent state (1797), amounts now to 133,000, of whom, how¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ever, one-fourth are paupers. Its trade has again been on the in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crease since the middle of the century, and Venice is now one of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the greatest seaports on the Adriatic.{% endraw %}
