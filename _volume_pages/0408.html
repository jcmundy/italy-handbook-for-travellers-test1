{% raw %}Aquileia, 18 M. to theW., once a most important Roman colony (founded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}B. C. 181), and at that period strongly fortified, was the principal bulwark of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Italy on the N.E. frontier. The population at the time of Augustus, who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frequently visited the town, is computed to have been 100,000. It was then{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the great centre of the traffic between Italy and the N. and E. of Europe,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and supplied the inhabitants of Illyria and Pannonia with grain, oil, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wine, in return for slaves and cattle. The incursions of the Romans into{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these districts were always undertaken from this point. In 452 Attila,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}exasperated by the obstinate resistance he encountered here, caused the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}city to be plundered and destroyed. The sole trace of its ancient glory{% endraw %}
