{% raw %}About V2 M. beyond the Porta d'Azeglio (PL D, 7), on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2nd road to the right, and near the Casa Minghetti, is situated the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church of S. Maria , called Mezzaratta , containing early Bolognese{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frescoes of little interest to the ordinary traveller. About 3/4 M.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}farther, on a height to the left of the road, rises S. Michele in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bosco (1437), once an Olivetan monastery (suppressed in 1797).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the church are remains of frescoes by Bagnacavallo and others.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The court is adorned with frescoes by the Carracci and their pupils,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the history of St. Benedict and St. Cecilia, unfortunately much{% endraw %}
{% raw %}injured. The monastery was converted into a royal chateau (Villa{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Reale) in 1860, and is now an Orthopaedic Institute. Fine view{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(cab, see p. 307). The visit to S. Michele may be combined with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that to the Certosa (see below), the whole round forming a beautiful{% endraw %}
{% raw %}drive of about 2 hours.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Outside the Porta S. Isaia (PL A, 4), at the W. end of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}town , is situated the *Certosa (formerly a Carthusian monastery),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected in 1335, and consecrated in 1801 as a Campo Santo. It{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occupies the site of an old Etruscan burial-ground, discovered here{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1869. The route to it is by the principal road from the gate; after{% endraw %}
{% raw %}9 min. a cross indicates the way to the cemetery, which is reached{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 5 min. more. — From the Porta Saragozza the Campo Santo is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reached by following the arcades of the Madonna di S. Luca to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}point where they divide (see below) and then turning to the right.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— The custodian of the cemetery is well-informed (fee Y2-I fr.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The church contains a few paintings by Elisabetta Sirani and others.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the beginning of the Cloisters are ancient tombstones from suppressed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}churches, arranged according to centuries: at the entrance 13th cent.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}then 15th on the right, 14th on the right (including monuments of pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fessors with scenes from their lecture-rooms), and 16th on the left; in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the arcades modern monuments, most of them in marble, including figures{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Faith by Galletti and Grief by Monari. In the centre are the ordinary{% endraw %}
{% raw %}graves. Among many illustrious names on the former are those of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}philologist Gaspar Garatoni (d. 1817) and the talented Clotilda Tambroni{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(d. 1817; p. 308); the principal families of the town also possess vaults{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here. Thus the monument of Letizia Mur at Pepoli (d. 1859), with a statue{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of her father King Murat ('propugnatore dell' italica indipendenza'), exe¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cuted by Vine. Vela. A rotunda here contains the busts of celebrated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}professors of the present century, Mezzofanti, Galvani, Costa, Schiassi,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mattel (teacher of Rossini), etc.{% endraw %}
