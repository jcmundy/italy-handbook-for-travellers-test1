{% raw %}XXXIV{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ITALIAN ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}underwent an independent and unbiassed development, and never{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entirely abandoned its ancient principles. A considerable interval{% endraw %}
{% raw %}indeed elapsed before the fusion of the original inhabitants with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the early mediaeval immigrants was complete, before the aggregate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of different tribes, languages, customs, and ideas became blended{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into a single nationality, and before the people attained sufficient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}concentration and independence of spirit to devote themselves{% endraw %}
{% raw %}successfully to the cultivation of art. Unproductive in the pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vince of art as this early period is, yet an entire departure from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}native tradition, or a serious conflict of the latter with extraneous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}innovation never took place. It may be admitted, that in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}massive columns and cumbrous capitals of the churches of Upper{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Italy, and in the art of vaulting which was developed here at an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}early period, symptoms of the Germanic character of the inhabi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tants are manifested, and that in the Lower Italian and especially{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sicilian structures, traces of Arabian and Norman influence are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unmistakable. In the essentials, however, the foreigners continue{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to be the recipients; the might of ancient tradition, and the natio¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nal idea of form could not be repressed or superseded.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About the middle of the 11th century a zealous and promis-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman- inS artistic movement took place in Italy, and the seeds{% endraw %}
{% raw %}esque were sown which three or four centuries later yielded so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Style, luxuriant a growth. As yet nothing was matured, nothing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}completed, the aim was obscure, the resources insufficient; mean¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while architecture alone satisfied artistic requirements, the at¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tempts at painting and sculpture being barbarous in the ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}treme ; these, however, were the germs of the subsequent devel¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}opment of art observable as early as the 11th and 12th centuries.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This has been aptly designated the Romanesque period (11th-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}13th cent.), and the then prevalent forms of art the Ro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manesque Style. As the Romance languages, notwithstanding{% endraw %}
{% raw %}alterations, additions, and corruptions, maintain their relation of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}daughtership to the language of the Romans, so Romanesque art,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in spite of its rude and barbarous aspect, reveals its immediate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}descent from the art of that people. The Tuscan towns were the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}principal scene of the prosecution of mediaeval art. There an in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dustrial population gradually arose, treasures of commerce were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}collected, independent views of life were acquired in active party-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conflicts, loftier common interests became interwoven with those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of private life, and education entered a broader and more enlight¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ened track; and thus a taste for art also was awakened and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aesthetic perception developed itself. When Italian architecture{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Romanesque period is examined, the difference between its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}character and that of contemporaneous northern works is at once{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apparent. In the latter the principal aim is perfection in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}construction of vaulting. French, English, and German churches{% endraw %}
