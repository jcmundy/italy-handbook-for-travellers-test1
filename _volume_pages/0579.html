{% raw %}The *Palazzo Pitti (PL C, 6), conspicuously situated on an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}eminence, was designed and begun by Brunelleschi in 1440, by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}order of Luca Pitti, the powerful opponent of the Medici, whom{% endraw %}
{% raw %}he hoped to excel in external grandeur by the erection of the most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}imposing palace yet built by a private citizen. The failure of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conspiracy against Piero de' Medici in 1466 cost Luca the loss of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his power and influence, and the building remained unfinished till{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the middle of the following cent., when it had come, through a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}great-grandson of Luca, into the possession of Eleonora, wife of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Duke Cosimo I. (1549). The palace , which somewhat resembles a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}castle or a prison, is remarkable for its bold simplicity, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unadorned blocks of stone are hewn smooth at the joints only. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}central part has a third story. The effectiveness of the building is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entirely produced by its fine proportions (comp. p. xlii). The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}length of the central part is 118 yds., its height 121 ft.; the whole{% endraw %}
{% raw %}facade is 220 yds. long. About the year 1568 Bartolommeo Am¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manati introduced round-arched windows on the ground floor, where{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the two portals, and the small rectangular windows, at a consider¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}able height from the ground, were originally the only openings.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the same time he began to construct the large court, which is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adjoined by a grotto with niches and fountains, and the Boboli Gar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}den beyond, them. The wings of the palace were completed in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1620-31. The two projecting wings were added in the 18th cen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tury. — Since the 16th cent, the Pitti Palace has been the resi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dence of the reigning sovereign, and is now that of King Humbert{% endraw %}
{% raw %}when at Florence. The upper floor of the left wing contains the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}far-famed **Picture Gallery, which was formerly the property of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cardinal Leopold and Carlo de' Medici, and of the Grand-duke{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ferdinand II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Pitti Gallery, which contains about 500 works, may be re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}garded as an extension of the Tribuna (p. 382) in the Uffizi gallery.{% endraw %}
