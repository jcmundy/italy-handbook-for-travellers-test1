{% raw %}The Maritime Alps and the immediately contiguous Apennines (the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}boundary between which is near Savona, about 20 M. to the W. of Genoa){% endraw %}
{% raw %}slope gently northwards to the Po in the form of an extensive rolling{% endraw %}
{% raw %}country, and descend abruptly towards the sea to the S. The narrow{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Riviera, or coast-district, expands at a few points only into small plains.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The cultivated land climbs up the hill-sides in terraces, sheltered from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}N. wind, and enjoying a fine sunny aspect. While the mean temperature{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Turin is 5373° Fahr., it is no less than 61° at Genoa; and again, while{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the temperature of January averages 31° at the former, and occasionally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}falls below zero, it averages 46° at the latter, and is rarely lower than{% endraw %}
{% raw %}23°. — The climate of the Riviera is therefore milder than that of Rome,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and is even favourable to the growth of the palm.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}As the country differs in many respects from Piedmont, so also do its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Inhabitants, while their Genoese dialect, which is difficult for foreigners{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to understand, occupies a middle place between the Gallic patois of Upper{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Italy and that of Sardinia. The historical development of the two countries{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has also been widely different. The natural resource of the Ligurians, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the inhabitants of the Riviera, was the sea, and they were accordingly known{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Greeks at a very early period as pirates and freebooters. To what{% endraw %}
{% raw %}race the Ligurians belong has not yet been ascertained. As the Greek{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Massalia formed the centre of trade in S. France, with Nice as its extreme{% endraw %}
{% raw %}outpost towards the E., so Genoa constituted the natural outlet for the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traffic of the Riviera. During the 3rd cent. B.C. Genoa became subject{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Romans, who in subsequent centuries had to wage long and obstinate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wars with the Ligurians, in order to secure the possession of the military{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coast-road to Spain. As late as the reign of Augustus the Roman culture{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had made little progress here. At that period the inhabitants exported{% endraw %}
{% raw %}timber, cattle, hides, wool, and honey, receiving wine and oil in exchange.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the 7th cent, the Lombards gained a footing here, and thenceforth the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}political state of the country was gradually altered. The W. part with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nice belonged to the Provence, but in 1388 came into the possession of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Counts of Savoy, forming their only access to the sea down to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period when they acquired Genoa (1815).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Var, which the Emp. Augustus had declared to be the boun¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dary between Italy and Gaul, continued to be so down to 1860, when, as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a reward for services rendered by Napoleon III., Italy ceded to France{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the districts of Savoy (4316 sq. M.) and Nice (1436 sq. M.). While the loss{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Savoy, though the cradle of the dynasty, was not severely felt owing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the notorious French sympathies and clerical propensities of the in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}habitants, the cession of Nice was regarded by the Italians as a national{% endraw %}
{% raw %}injury. The E. part of the Riviera now forms the Province of Porto{% endraw %}
