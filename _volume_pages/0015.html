{% raw %}INTRODUCTION.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Thou art the garden of the world, the home{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Of all Art yields, and Nature can decree;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}E'en in thy desert, what is like to thee?{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thy very weeds are beautiful, thy waste{% endraw %}
{% raw %}More rich than other climes'1 fertility,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thy wreck a glory, and thy ruin graced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}With an immaculate charm which cannot be defaced.''{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Byron.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}I. Travelling Expenses. Money.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Expenses. The cost of a tour in Italy depends of course on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller's resources and habits, but, as a rule, it need not exceed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that incurred in other much frequented parts of the continent. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}average expenditure of a single traveller, when in Italy, may be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}estimated at 25-30 francs per day, or at 12-15 francs when a pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}longed stay is made at one place; but persons acquainted with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}language and habits of the country may easily restrict their ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}penses to still narrower limits. Those who travel as members of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}party effect a considerable saving by sharing the expense of guides,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carriages, and other items. When ladies are of the party, the ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}penses are generally greater.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Money. The French monetary system is now in use throughout{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the whole of Italy. The franc (lira or franco) contains 100 centesimi;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1 fr. 25c. = 1 s. = 1 German mark = 50 Austrian kreutzers. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}copper (bronzo or rame) there are coins of 1, 2, 5, and 10 centesimi.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A piece of 5 c. is called a soldo, or sou, and as the lower classes of¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ten keep their accounts in soldi, the traveller will find it useful to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accustom himself to this mode of reckoning. See also the Money{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Table opposite the title-page.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}During the war of 1866 a paper currency was introduced at a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}compulsory rate of exchange, and for many years gold and silver{% endraw %}
{% raw %}almost completely disappeared from ordinary circulation. In 1880,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}however, a measure was passed for the gradual redemption of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}banknotes, and the government loan of 450 million francs for this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}purpose has been taken up with such avidity, that the extreme{% endraw %}
{% raw %}date fixed for the complete substitution of a gold currency (the end{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of 1883) will probably be anticipated. The exchange in favour of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gold, which formerly amounted to 8-10 or even to 15 per cent.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has already sunk to 1V2-3 per cent. The only banknotes which are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}current throughout the whole country are those of the Banca{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nazionale and the so-called Biglietti gia Consorziali, formerly issued{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in common by six banks but now taken over by the government.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Other notes should be refused.{% endraw %}
