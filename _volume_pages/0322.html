{% raw %}Immediately to the S. of the Arena stands the Municipio (PL D,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4; formerly the guard-house), begun in 1836, adjoining which is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Gran Guardia Antica (PL D, 4; now the corn-magazine and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}agricultural exhibition), or old guard-house, begun in 1609 by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dom. Curtoni, a nephew of Sammicheli. Adjacent is the Portone,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an old gate with a tower. Opposite the Municipio is the spacious{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Palazzo Sparavieri, formerly Guastaverza (by Sammicheli), with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the cafes mentioned at p. 199.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the street to the right of the door is the Teatro Filarmonico{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL C, 4). In the court towards the Piazza Bra, under the ar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cades erected by Pompei in 1745, is situated the valuable Museo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lapidario, collected and described by Scipione Maffei, containing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman, Greek, and Arabic inscriptions, and ancient and mediaeval{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sculptures. The custodian lives in a side-street at the back of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}theatre, No. 7.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Passing through the gate we reach the Corso Vittorio Emma¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nuele (PL C, B, 4, 5), in which, at the corner of the Strada di S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Antonio, is a modern Statue of Michele Sammicheli, 'grande nella{% endraw %}
{% raw %}architettura civile e religiosa, massimo nella militare', by Trojani.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the end of the Corso stands the Porta Nuova (PL B, 6), a fine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}work by Sammicheli. Outside this gate lies the Stazione Porta Nuova{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 199).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the Porta Nuova an avenue of trees leads N.W. to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*Porta Stuppa (or del Palio; PL A, 4), the finest of the gates of Ve¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rona, erected by Sammicheli. We then follow the Corso di Porta Palio,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and turn into the second cross-street on the left, in which stands —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Bernardino (PL A, 3; entrance from the E. corner through{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a pleasing court; if the church-door is closed, ring in the corner to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the left, adjoining the church), a former monastery-church of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}15th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Interior. The 4th chapel to the right is adorned with ceiling-frescoes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and scenes from the life of St. Anthony by Domen. Morone. The Cappella{% endraw %}
{% raw %}della Croce (5th to the right) contains (on the right) a Lazarus by Badile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Cure of those possessed with devils by Paolo Veronese, and (on the left){% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christ taking leave of his mother by Caroio, and paintings by Giolfino.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the high-altar is a Crucifixion by Fr. Morone (1698). To the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the high-altar is the entrance to the "Cappella dei Pellegrini, by Sam¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}micheli, one of the finest of the circular buildings of the Renaissance,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the antique forms cleverly and beautifully executed. In the choir,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the left, is a Madonna with saints, by Benaglio. The nave is adorned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with figures of SS. Bernardino and Francis (to the left) and of SS. Bona-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ventura and Ludovico (over the portal), executed by Moroni or Liberate.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the N. of this point lies *S. Zeno Maggiore (PL A, 2) the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}finest Romanesque church in N. Italy, distinguished for its noble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}proportions. The nave in its present form was begun in 1139, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}choir dates from the 13th century. The church was lately restored.{% endraw %}
