{% raw %}On the side next the Lagune the Murazzi are perpendicular,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while towards the sea they descend in four terraces. The Murazzi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the Lido from Pelestrina to Chioggia (p. 277) elate from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}last period of the republic. The Diga of Malamocco, a pier which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extends for a distance of 11/4 M. into the open sea, was constructed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Austrian government after 1825, in order to prevent the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}harbour from becoming choked with mud. The Lagune are connected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the open sea by means of four entrances, of which those of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Lido and Malamocco alone are available for vessels of heavy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tonnage.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Lagoons are called either 'lagune vive\ or '■lagune morte\{% endraw %}
{% raw %}about one half of them belonging to each class. In the former the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tide rises and falls about 2 ft.; the latter, shallower, and situated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nearer the mainland, are unaffected by the tide. Venice is situated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the 'laguna viva'. At high water innumerable stakes, protrud¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing from the water in groups of the most varied form, mark the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situation and shape of the low sand-islands which surround the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}city on every side, forming a complicated network of navigable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}channels, most of them accessible to small boats only.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Most of the houses rise immediately from the canals (rii), or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are separated from them by narrow streets only, here called (as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Spain) colli (sing, il calle'), and paved with broad slabs of stone,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or sometimes with brick or asphalt. These lanes form a laby¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rinth from which the stranger will frequently find it difficult to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extricate himself; none, however, but walkers can form an adequate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acquaintance with the picturesque nooks of the city and the char¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acteristics of its inhabitants. The following description is so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arranged that many of the sights can be visited on foot, but all{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the principal buildings may also be visited by boat. Gondola-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}travelling is very pleasant, and is of course far preferable to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walking for expeditions of any length.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The **Piazza of St. Mark, usually called 'La Piazza'' (the other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}open spaces being ' campi"1), is a square paved with blocks of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trachyte and marble, 192yds. in length, and on theW. side 61, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the E. 90 yds. in breadth. On three sides it is enclosed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}imposing structures, which appear to form one vast marble palace,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}blackened by age and exposure to the weather; on the E. it is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bounded by the Church of St. Mark and the Piazzetta (p. 242).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}These palaces were once the residence of the nine 'procurators', the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}highest officials of the republic after the Doge, whence their appel¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lation of Procuratle. The Procuratie Vecchie, or N. wing, were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected at the close of the 15th cent, by Bartolommeo Buon. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Procuratie Nuove, or S. wing, were begun by Scamozzi in 1584.{% endraw %}
