{% raw %}— We now return and enter the Armoury to the right.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Via Alessandro Manzoni leads hence to the right to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Via Bigli, in which stands the Casa Taverno or Ponti, with a fine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portal and an admirably restored court of the 16th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We next proceed from the Piazza della Scala to the N. by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Via S. Giuseppe (PI. E, 4) and Via di Brera to the Brera. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Via del Monte di Pieta, the second side-street on the left, is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}handsome new Cassa di Risparmio, or savings-bank, by Balzaretti.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Brera (PI. E, 3; Via di Brera 28), or Palazzo delle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Scienze ed Arti, formerly a Jesuits' College, contains the Picture{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gallery, the Library of the Academy founded in 1170 (200,000 vols.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}open daily), a Collection of Coins (50,000), the Observatory, a col¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lection of Casts from the antique, and an Archaeological Museum.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the centre of the handsome Court by Richini is a bronze{% endraw %}
{% raw %}statue of*Napoleon I., as aRoman emperor, by Canova, considered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one of his finest works. By the staircase, to the left, the statue{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the celebrated jurist Beccaria (d. 1794), who was the first to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}call in question the justice of capital punishment. The courtisalso{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adorned with several other statues.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Picttjre Gallery (Pinacoteca), which contains about 600{% endraw %}
{% raw %}works, is open daily from 9 a.m. to 4 p.m. (on holidays from 12,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in winter and on Sundays till 3); admission 1 fr., Sundays and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thursdays gratis (catalogue l1/^ fr.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The gem of the collection is Raphael's Sposalizio (No. 270),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the chief work of his first or Umbrian period. The numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pictures of the Lombard school, and particularly the frescoes sawn{% endraw %}
{% raw %}out of churches, are also very valuable. The drawing of the head{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Christ for the Last Supper (No. 267) shows with what beauty{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Leonardo could invest his figures. Among the oil-paintings,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}No. 265 by Bernardino Luini is a very meritorious work, and among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the frescoes, Nos. 47 and 52, by the same master. The most in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}teresting works of the early Italian school are No. 159 by Gentile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}da Fabriano, and No. 264 by Mantegna. The collection also affords{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an instructive survey of the progress of Carlo Crivelli (who flourish¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed in 1468-93; 2nd room), a master who connects the Paduan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}school with that of Venice. The most notable works of the latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}school are No. 166 by Gentile Bellini, Nos. 284 and 261 by Gio¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vanni Bellini, and No. 300 by Cima da Conegliano; and of a later{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period No. 209 by Bonifacio , No. 248 by Titian, and Nos. 253,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}254, 255 by Lorenzo Lotto. No. 456 by Domenichino, and No. 331{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Guercino , represent the Italian masters of the 17th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The most important works of foreign schools are No. 447 by Rubens,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nos. 442 and 440 by Van Dyck, and No. 449 by Rembrandt. Each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}picture bears the name of the painter.{% endraw %}
