{% raw %}BERGAMO. 30. Route. 183{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bergamo. —Hotels. * Albergo d'Italia, R. from 2, B. 172fr.;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cappello d'Oro , unpretending. — Trattoria Giardinetto, by the Porta S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Agostino, with garden and view. Gaffe Centrale. — Cabs: 272fr. per hour.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bergamo (1246 ft.), the ancient Bergomum, which belonged to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the republic of Venice from 1428 to 1797, is now a provincial{% endraw %}
{% raw %}capital with 22,700 inhab. (commune 39,700), and one of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}busiest of the smaller trading and manufacturing towns in Italy.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The once far-famed fair (Fiera di S. Alessandro, lasting from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}middle of August to the middle of September) has now lost its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}importance. The town consists of two distinct parts, the old and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the new. The New Town (Borgo S. Leonardo and Borgo S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tomaso), with its woollen, silk, and other manufactories, the Corso,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the interesting piazza where the fair is held, the new Prefettura,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and a recently-completed Protestant church, lies in the plain.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the railway-station a wide street leads to the Piazza Ca¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vour, whence a narrow street runs to the left to the church of S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alessandro in Colonna (PL 12; C, 4), containing a fine Assump¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion by Romanino. The Contrada Torquato Tasso leads to the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the Piazza Cavour to S. Bartolommeo (with a *Madonna by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lotto in the choir, 1516) and to S. Spirito, a beautiful Renaissance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}building without aisles. Over the 2nd altar to the left is a Ma¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}donna by Borgognone (1508), and by the 3rd altar to the right is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*Madonna by Lotto (1521). — Farther on, in the Contrada di Pignolo,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are the churches of S. Bernardino (*Lotto, Madonna and Saints,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1521) and S. Alessandro della Croce (Lotto, Christ enthroned).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Old Town (Citta), beautifully situated on the hills and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}containing many interesting houses of the early and late Renaissance,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is connected with the lower town by the Strada Vittorio Emmanuele.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Promenade affords a fine view of the Brianza (p. 149), and of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the beautiful amphitheatre formed by the surrounding mountains,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}particularly those to the N.E. The Castle (PL A, 1), on the hill to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the N.W., commands a still finer prospect.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Piazza Garibaldi, or market-place (11/4 M. from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}railway-station), is situated the Palazzo Nuovo (PL 8; C, 2),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the seat of the municipal authorities, erected in the Renaissance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}style by Scamozzi, but unfinished. Opposite to it is the library in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Gothic Palazzo Vecchio , or Broletto , the ground-floor of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}consists of an open hall supported by pillars and columns. Near it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are the Monument of Torquato Tasso (whose father Bernardo was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}born at Bergamo in 1493), and a handsome fountain.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the back of the Broletto is the Romanesque church of S. Maria{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Maggiore (PL 6; B, C, 2, 3), erected in 1173, with ancient por¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tals supported by lions on the N. and S. sides. Adjoining the N.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portal is the rich Renaissance facade of the chapel of the Colleoni.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Interior (entrance on the S. side) contains some ancient pictures,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fine *Carved work on the choir-stalls by the Bergamasque Giov. Franc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Capo Ferrato, and admirable inlaid wood (intarsia) by Fra Damiano. This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church also contains the monument of the celebrated composer Donizetti{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Bergamo (d. 1848), by Vine Vela, and, opposite, that of his teacher{% endraw %}
