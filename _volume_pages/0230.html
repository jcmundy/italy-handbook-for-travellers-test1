{% raw %}Milan (390 ft.), surnamed '■la grande', the Mediolanum of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Romans, which was rebuilt after its total destruction in 1162 by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Erap. Frederick Barbarossa, is the capital of Lombardy, the seat{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of an archbishop, the headquarters of an army-corps, and one of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wealthiest manufacturing towns in Italy, silk and woollen goods{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being the staple commodities. It is situated on the small river{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Olona, which however is navigable and is connected by means of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Naviglio Grande (p. 79) with the Ticino and Lago Maggiore, by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Naviglio di Pavia with the Ticino and the Po, and by the Na¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}viglio della Martesana with the Adda, the Lake of Como, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Po. The town is 7 M. in circumference, and contains upwards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of 200,000 inhab., or, including the suburbs, 321,600.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The favourable situation of Milan in the centre of Lombardy has al¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ways secured for it a high degree of prosperity. Under the Romans it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was one of the largest cities in Italy (p. 126), but owing to its repeated de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}struction hardly a trace of that period has been left. In the llth cent.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it contained 300,000 inhabitants. Its heroic struggles against the German{% endraw %}
{% raw %}emperors are well known. With the exception of S. Ambrogio and a few{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other churches , the city was totally destroyed in 1162 by the emperor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Frederick Barbarossa, but in 1167 it was rebuilt by the allied cities of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Brescia, Bergamo, Mantua, and Verona. It was afterwards ruled by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Visconti (1312-1447), then by the Sforza family (1447-1535). Under the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}supremacy of the latter it attained the zenith of its reputation as a patron{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of art, having been the residence of Bramante from 1476 to 1500, and of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Leonardo da Vinci from 1494 to 1516. The most eminent of Leonardo's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pupils who flourished here were Bernardino Luini, Cesare da Sesto, Giov.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ant. Boltraffio, Marco da Oggionno, Andrea Saldino, and Gaudenzio Ferrari.{% endraw %}
