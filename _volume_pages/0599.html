{% raw %}446 Route 54. VILLA CAREGGI Environs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}{Casino delle Cascine, a cafe'-restaurant like Doney's, p. 365), a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gay and favourite resort towards sunset. Beyond this spot the park{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is comparatively deserted, and it terminates about 1 M. farther,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where the large monument of the Rajah of Kohlapore, who died at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Florence in 1870, and whose body was burnt at this spot, is situated.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— Tramway from Piazza Nuova di S. Maria Novella to the Cascine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}every 5 min. ; steam-tramway every 2 hrs. (p. 367).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the same road lies Poggio a Cajano (tramway every 2 hours{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from Piazza Nuova di S. Maria Novella, see above; a pleasant drive of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ll/z hr.; permessi for the villa in the Palazzo Pitti). The village con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tains two very unpretending inns (Italia and Falcone). At the end of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the village stands the Villa, built for Lorenzo il Magnifico by Giuliano{% endraw %}
{% raw %}da Sangallo in a simple rustic style, and still entirely without modern{% endraw %}
{% raw %}additions. It is surrounded by a fine old park and commands a beautiful{% endraw %}
{% raw %}view of the Tuscan mountains. It is now a royal possession. The chief{% endraw %}
{% raw %}room of the 2nd story is adorned with frescoes by Andrea del Sarto{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Ceesar receiving the tribute of Egypt), Franciabigio (Triumph of Cicero){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Aless. Allori (Flaminius in Greece, and Scipio in the house of Syphax,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1580); the subjects are supposed to typify events in the history of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Medici as narrated by Giovio. — The loggia has a fine stucco-ceilng.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From Piazza della Stazione (PI. D, 3) the tramway (p. 367) proceeds{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Ponte a Rifredi (railway-station, see p. 364). Near the ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church of S. Stefano in Pane, lies the Villa Careggi, the property of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}grand-dukes down to 1780, afterwards that of the Orsi family, and now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}owned by Count Buturlin. The villa was erected by Michelozzo for the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first Cosimo, who terminated his brilliant career at this house in 1464.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This was also once the seat of the Platonic Academy (p. 373). Lorenzo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}il Magnifico, grandson of Cosimo, also died at Careggi (1492). Fine view{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the environs. A few frescoes by Pontormo and Bronzino and a series of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portraits are reminiscences of the history of this edifice.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Farther W. is the Villa della Petraia, with delightful gardens, erected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Buontalenti, and adorned with paintings by Andrea del Sarto and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Daniele da Volterra. The villa, which is now fitted up as a royal resi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dence, is most conveniently reached from the railway-station Castello{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 364). Near it is the Villa Quarto, with beautiful gardens, formerly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the property of the Medici, now that of Countess Stroganoff.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From railway-stat. Sesto (p. 364) a visit may be paid to the great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}porcelain-manufactory della Doccia, the property of Marchese Ginori, who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}possesses a villa here. The proprietor has successfully revived the ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}majolica manufacture, and produces excellent copies of Della Robbia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}work (permessi in the depot of the factory, in the Via Rondinelli). — By{% endraw %}
{% raw %}taking the tramway to Sesto, we pass the villa of Marchese Corsi, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}celebrated grower and exporter of plants, which is interesting to botanists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and horticulturalists (permessi at Via Tornabuoni 20, or Via deiPescioni 5).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(H.) Fiesole, on the height to the N. of Florence, is 3 M.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distant (omnibus, starting from the little Piazza del Capitolo, to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. of the Piazza del Duomo, three times daily, lfr. ; carr. 7-8 fr.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stipulate for a visit to the Badia; see p. 448). We leave the town{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Porta S. Gallo (PL H, I, 1), where a Triumphal Arch of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}no artistic merit, erected in 1738, commemorates the entry of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}grand-duke Francis II. Outside the gate rises a handsome new{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colonnade. — We follow the Viale Militare (PI. I, 1), cross the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Piazza delle Cure, and then ascend the left bank of the Mugnone{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Via Boccaccio), an insignificant stream, which however in rainy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}weather sometimes causes great devastation. In about 20 min. the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Villa Palmieri, now the property of the Earl of Crawford, is passed{% endraw %}
