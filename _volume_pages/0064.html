{% raw %}The condition of Italian art, that of painting at least, improved{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to some extent towards the close of the 16th century, when there{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was a kind of second efflorescence, known in the schools as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}-ival^ the 'revival of good taste', which is said to have chiefly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manifested itself in two directions, the eclectic and the na¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}turalistic. But these are terms of little or no moment in the study{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of art, and the amateur had better disregard them. This period of art{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also should be studied historically. The principal architectural mon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}uments of the 17th century are the churches of the Jesuits, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unquestionably produce a most imposing effect; but the historical{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enquirer will not easily be dazzled by their meretricious magni¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ficence. He will perceive the absence of organic forms and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}impropriety of combining totally different styles, and he will steel{% endraw %}
{% raw %}himself against the gorgeous, but monotonous attractions of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paintings and other works of the same period. The bright Renais¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sance is extinct, simple pleasure in the natural and human is ob¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}literated. A gradual change in the views of the Italian public and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the position of the church did not fail to influence the tendencies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of art, and in the 17th century artists again devoted their energies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}more immediately to the service of the church. Devotional pictures{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now became more frequent, but at the same time a sensual,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}naturalistic element gained ground. At one time it veils itself in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beauty of form, at another it is manifested in the representation of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}voluptuous and passionate emotions; classic dignity and noble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}symmetry are never attained. Crist. Allori's Judith should be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}compared with the beauties of Titian, and the frescoes of Annibale{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Carracci in the Palazzo Farnese with Raphael's ceiling-paintings in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Farnesina, in OTder that the difference between the 16th and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}17th centuries may be clearly understood; and the enquirer will be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still farther aided by consulting the coeval Italian poetry, and ob¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}serving the development of the lyric drama or opera. The poetry of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the period thus furnishes a key to the mythological representations{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the School of the Carracci. Gems of art, however, were not un-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frequ entry produced during the 17th century, and many of the frescoes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of this period are admirable, such as those by Guido Reni and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Domenichino at Rome. Beautiful oil-paintings by various masters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are also preserved in the Italian galleries. Besides the public col¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lections of Bologna, Naples, and the Vatican and Capitol, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}private galleries of Rome are of great importance. The so-called{% endraw %}
